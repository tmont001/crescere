## What You Will Learn This Week

Week 6 takes you into one of the most practical — and most French — scenarios there is: the café. You will learn to order confidently, navigate a restaurant menu, and hold a basic conversation with a waiter in French.

You will also learn partitive articles: the little words (*du, de la, de l', des*) that French uses where English just says "some" — or nothing at all. They show up constantly in food contexts and will become second nature by the end of the week.

---

## What You Will Be Able to Say by the End of Week 6

By the end of Session 12, you should be able to:

**At a café:**
> *Bonjour. Je voudrais un café crème et une tartine, s'il vous plaît. Merci.*

**At a restaurant:**
> *Bonsoir. Nous voudrions une table pour deux. ... Je vais prendre le menu à vingt euros. Pour l'entrée, je prends la salade, et comme plat principal, je voudrais le poulet. Est-ce que vous pouvez me recommander un dessert? ... L'addition, s'il vous plaît.*

**Expressing preferences:**
> *J'adore le fromage. Je mange du fromage tous les jours. Mais je ne mange pas de viande — je suis végétarien.*

And answer spontaneously:
- *Qu'est-ce que vous prenez?* — *Je voudrais un thé vert, s'il vous plaît.*
- *Vous prenez du dessert?* — *Non merci, je ne prends pas de dessert.*

## Partitive Articles — "Some" in French

English often says "I want coffee" with no article. French always makes the "some" explicit:

| French says | Instead of | Used before |
|---|---|---|
| Je veux **du** café. | — | masculine noun |
| Je veux **de la** bière. | — | feminine noun |
| Je veux **de l'**eau. | — | noun starting with vowel or silent h |
| Je veux **des** croissants. | — | any plural noun |

**The negation rule — the most important grammar point this week:**

After *ne...pas*, all four forms collapse to **de** (or **d'** before a vowel):

- *Je prends du café.* → *Je ne prends **pas de** café.*
- *Il mange de la tarte.* → *Il ne mange **pas de** tarte.*
- *Elle boit de l'eau.* → *Elle ne boit **pas d'**eau.*

There are no exceptions. After negation: always *de*.

## Je Voudrais — Your Most Important Phrase This Week

*Je voudrais* means "I would like." It is polite, natural, and used constantly in French cafés and restaurants.

Technically it is a conditional form — a tense you will study fully in A2. For now, treat it as a fixed phrase.

- *Je voudrais un café, s'il vous plaît.*
- *Je voudrais de la tarte.*
- *Nous voudrions une table pour deux.*

You can also say *je vais prendre...* (I'll have...) or *je prends...* (I'm having...). All are correct; *je voudrais* is the most polite.

## The Menu vs. La Carte

One false friend to learn now:

| French | What it means |
|---|---|
| **le menu** | A *fixed-price* meal deal (starter + main, or main + dessert, or all three at a set price) |
| **la carte** | The full list of individual dishes you can choose from |
| **le plat du jour** | The dish of the day — usually the best value |

When a French person says *"Je vais prendre le menu,"* they mean the set meal — not that they want to look at the menu. If you want to see the menu, ask: *"Vous avez la carte?"*

And: **l'entrée in French = the starter, not the main course.** The main course is *le plat principal.*

## Vouloir — Key Conjugation This Week

| Subject | Vouloir |
|---|---|
| je | **veux** |
| tu | **veux** |
| il / elle / on | **veut** |
| nous | **voulons** |
| vous | **voulez** |
| ils / elles | **veulent** |

*Je voudrais* is derived from *vouloir* — the conditional form you will learn in A2. Learn it now as a fixed phrase.

## What to Practice Between Sessions

**After Session 11 — before Session 12:**

1. **Say the partitive forms aloud.** *Du café, de la bière, de l'eau, des croissants.* Then say the negatives: *Pas de café, pas de bière, pas d'eau, pas de croissants.*
2. **Write your café order.** What would you order at a French café right now? Write it using *je voudrais.*
3. **Say it out loud.** Third try: no notes.

**After Session 12 — before Session 13:**

1. **Write a mini restaurant dialogue.** 6–8 lines. You are the customer; write both sides.
2. **Practice the bill-asking phrase.** *L'addition, s'il vous plaît.* Say it ten times. It should be automatic.
3. **Learn one food item that surprises you.** Why is *une entrée* a starter? Why is *le menu* not just the menu? Cultural knowledge matters.

## A Note on Mistakes This Week

Two mistakes are very common — and very correctable:

1. **Forgetting to change partitive to *de* after negation.** You will say *"pas du café"* at some point. Your instructor will correct it every time. After a few corrections, it sticks.
2. **Saying *je veux* instead of *je voudrais.*** Both are grammatically correct, but *je veux* sounds blunt in a formal setting. In class you can use either; in a real French café, *je voudrais* is the better habit.

Neither of these is a big deal. The goal this week is to get through a real café or restaurant interaction in French. Everything else is refinement.
