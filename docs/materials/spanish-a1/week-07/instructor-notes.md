# Spanish A1 — Week 7 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 13 and 14
**Theme:** Shopping, Clothing, and the Market

---

## Week Objective

By the end of Week 7, students can:
- Use demonstrative adjectives (*este/esta/estos/estas* and *ese/esa/esos/esas*) correctly with clothing vocabulary
- Apply color adjective agreement (gender and number) with clothing nouns
- Ask prices using *¿Cuánto cuesta? / ¿Cuánto cuestan?* and understand simple responses
- Navigate a clothing shop interaction: finding an item, asking for a size, trying it on, buying or declining
- Use quantity expressions (*un kilo de, una botella de, una docena de...*) at a market
- Complete a full market role play: requesting items, specifying quantities, asking prices, completing a purchase

**Relationship to the curriculum:** This is exactly the Week 7 content specified in the curriculum. Session 13 = clothing, este/ese demonstratives, colors, shopping dialogue. Session 14 = market scenario, quantity expressions, prices. Week 6 content (*gustar, querer*) is closed.

**Demonstratives note:** The curriculum introduces *este/esta* and *ese/esa* series in Session 13 and *aquel/aquella* for recognition. Prioritize active production of *este* and *ese*; treat *aquel* as recognition only this week.

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 7

| Mistake | Correction | When to address |
|---|---|---|
| *Este camisa* (wrong gender) | *Esta camisa.* Camisa is feminine — *esta.* Students must learn clothing noun genders. | Session 13 demonstratives block |
| *Esta pantalón* (wrong gender) | *Este pantalón.* Pantalón is masculine. Note: *pantalón* is masculine even though many items you wear are feminine. | Session 13 demonstratives block |
| *Un vestido roja* (agreement error) | *Un vestido rojo.* Vestido is masculine — the color must match: *rojo.* | Session 13 colors block |
| *Unas zapatillas azulas* (wrong plural suffix) | *Unas zapatillas azules.* Colors ending in a consonant add -es: *azul → azules, gris → grises.* | Session 13 colors block |
| *Un kilo del queso* (keeping article after quantity) | *Un kilo de queso.* After quantity expressions, the article disappears — just *de.* | Session 14 quantities block |
| *¿Cuánto cuestan esta camisa?* (plural verb for singular subject) | *¿Cuánto **cuesta** esta camisa?* One item = *cuesta*; multiple items = *cuestan.* | Session 14 prices block |
| Ser/estar errors with clothing descriptions | *Esta camisa **está** muy cara* (current price — temporary) vs. *Esta camisa **es** de buena calidad* (inherent quality). Both occur naturally in a shop. Continue to correct. | Every time |
| *Naranja → naranjas* (incorrect plural of invariable color) | *Naranja* as a color is invariable — *unos pantalones naranja,* not *naranjas.* | Session 13 colors block |

## Suggested Instructor Language

- **For este vs. ese:** *"'Este' and 'esta' point to things that are near you — things you could touch. 'Ese' and 'esa' point to things farther away — across the room, or on a shelf. Think of 'este' as 'this' and 'ese' as 'that.' In a shop: 'Esta camisa' — the one I'm holding. 'Esa camisa' — the one on the rack over there."*

- **For color adjective agreement:** *"The color must match the noun in gender AND number. Ask yourself: what is the thing? If it's feminine, the color is feminine. If it's plural, the color is plural. So: 'un jersey azul' (one jersey), 'dos jerseys azules' (two jerseys). Most colors add -a for feminine and -s or -es for plural. But some — naranja, rosa, beige — never change at all."*

- **For quantity + de:** *"After any quantity word — un kilo, media docena, una botella — the article disappears. It's just 'de' plus the noun. So: 'un kilo de tomates,' 'una botella de agua.' Don't say 'un kilo de los tomates' — the definite article *los* goes away."*

- **For the shopping role play:** *"In a Spanish shop, the assistant will say '¿Le puedo ayudar?' or '¿En qué le puedo ayudar?' — 'Can I help you?' Your answer: 'Busco...' (I'm looking for...) or 'Solo estoy mirando' (I'm just browsing). When you want to try something: '¿Me lo puedo probar?' — Can I try it on? When you decide to buy: 'Me lo llevo.' When you don't: 'No, gracias, no me lo llevo.'"*

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- *Aquel/aquella/aquellos/aquellas* — recognition is enough this week; do not drill production
- More than 12 clothing items — pick the most common and use them actively
- Regional clothing vocabulary variants beyond one mention
- Complex price arithmetic (change-giving) — students need to understand and say prices, not do mental arithmetic
- Extended market food vocabulary — 10–12 items is enough for a realistic role play

## Cultural Context for Instructors

- **El mercado vs. el supermercado:** Open-air markets (*mercados*) are fundamental to shopping culture in Spain and Latin America. In Spain, covered mercados (*el Mercado de la Boquería* in Barcelona, *el Mercado de San Miguel* in Madrid) are major cultural landmarks. In Mexico, *el tianguis* is an outdoor market, often weekly. In Colombia, *la plaza de mercado* is where fresh produce is bought. Students should know that market shopping is not just for tourists.
- **Regional vocabulary:** *Patata* (Spain) vs. *papa* (Latin America); *vaqueros* (Spain) vs. *jeans* (widely understood); *el bolso* (Spain) vs. *la cartera / la bolsa* (Latin America). Flag as you go; do not overwhelm students with exhaustive regional lists.
- **Bargaining:** Bargaining is common in open markets across Latin America and in souk-style contexts. In Spain, bargaining is less common in formal shops but negotiating at open-air market stalls is acceptable. Students should know *¿Me hace un descuento?* (Can you give me a discount?) for informal markets.
- **Sizing:** Spanish clothing sizes follow European conventions (36, 38, 40, 42...). Students may also encounter S/M/L/XL. Latin American sizing varies; U.S. sizes are often understood in tourist areas.
