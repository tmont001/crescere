# Spanish A1 — Week 9 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 17 and 18
**Theme:** Plans and Obligations

---

## Week Objective

By the end of Week 9, students can:
- Use *ir a + infinitivo* to express near-future plans in speech
- Use *pensar + infinitivo* to express intentions (softer than *ir a*)
- Use common future time expressions fluently (*mañana, esta noche, la semana que viene, en dos días*)
- Use *tener que + infinitivo* to express personal obligation
- Use *hay que + infinitivo* to express general/impersonal obligation
- Distinguish *tener que* (personal) from *hay que* (general)
- Use *poder + infinitivo* in planning contexts (reviewed from Week 6)
- Combine all three structures in real scheduling conversations
- Conduct a short planning role play: suggesting, accepting, declining, and negotiating times

**Relationship to the curriculum:** This is exactly the Week 9 content specified in the curriculum. Session 17 = *ir a + infinitivo* + *pensar + infinitivo* + future time expressions. Session 18 = *tener que / hay que / poder* + combined planning scenarios.

**Prior knowledge students bring:** *Poder* and *querer* (Week 6); *tener* conjugation (Week 5); *hay* (Week 8). *Ir* is introduced for the first time in Session 17 — begin with a full paradigm presentation (voy/vas/va/vamos/vais/van) before moving to *ir a + infinitivo*. *Pensar* (e→ie stem change) and *hay que* are also new this week.

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 9

| Mistake | Correction | When to address |
|---|---|---|
| *Voy a ir al cine* — is this right? | Yes — *ir a ir* is grammatically possible and normal in speech. *Vamos a ir* / *voy a ir* are standard. Don't correct this. | No correction needed; mention if students flag it |
| *Voy trabajar mañana* (dropping *a*) | *Voy a trabajar mañana.* The *a* is mandatory: *ir + a + infinitivo.* | Session 17 immediately |
| *Hay que yo estudiar* (adding a subject) | *Hay que estudiar.* *Hay que* is impersonal — never takes a subject. To personalize: *tengo que estudiar.* | Session 18 hay que block |
| *Tengo que de trabajar* (adding *de*) | *Tengo que trabajar.* No preposition between *tener que* and the infinitive. | Session 18 tener que block |
| *No puedes venir?* (using *tú* form to ask a yes/no question) | Fine grammatically — in Latin American Spanish, yes/no questions can rise in intonation without inversion. Accept in class; note that formal written Spanish uses question marks at both ends: *¿No puedes venir?* | If it arises |
| *Yo quero ir* (mispelling/mispronouncing) | *Quiero ir.* The stem change: *e→ie* in yo: *quiero.* | Session 18 if *querer* comes up in combining block |
| *La semana próxima / la semana que viene* — which to use? | Both are correct and interchangeable. *La semana que viene* is more colloquial (Spain); *la semana próxima* is more neutral. Use either. | Session 17 time expressions block |
| *Pienso a llamarle* (wrong preposition) | *Pienso llamarle.* *Pensar + infinitivo* — no preposition. | Session 17 pensar block |

## Suggested Instructor Language

- **For *ir a + infinitivo*:** *"Ir is one of the most important irregular verbs in Spanish — and we're learning it now. The conjugation is: voy, vas, va, vamos, vais, van. Nothing looks like the infinitive ir, so memorize these six. Now: once you have the conjugation, near-future is simple — just add a + infinitivo after it. Voy a trabajar mañana. Vamos a cenar esta noche. Voy a llamarte mañana. That's it. Every native speaker uses this structure every single day."*

- **For the difference between *ir a* and *pensar + inf*:** *"'Voy a ir al gimnasio' — I'm going to go to the gym (fairly definite). 'Pienso ir al gimnasio' — I'm thinking of going to the gym (softer, more of an intention). At this level, don't overthink it. Use *ir a* when you're fairly sure; use *pienso* when you're still not certain."*

- **For *tener que*:** *"Tener que — you have to. It's personal. 'Tengo que trabajar mañana' means specifically me, not everyone. The same tener you know from Week 5: tengo, tienes, tiene... just add 'que' + infinitive after. Think of 'tener que' as one fixed unit — it always stays together."*

- **For *hay que* vs. *tener que*:** *"Hay que is general — it's like saying 'one must' or 'you have to' in the general sense. No subject, never changes: 'Hay que estudiar' — it's necessary to study. If you want to say YOU specifically have to, use tener que: 'Tengo que estudiar.' Hay que is about rules or general necessity; tener que is about your personal obligation."*

- **For combining modals:** *"Three tools — three jobs: 'tengo que' = I have to (obligation), 'quiero' = I want to (desire), 'puedo' = I can (ability). In real scheduling conversations, you'll use all three: 'Quiero venir, pero tengo que trabajar. ¿Puedes el sábado?' That's four words of grammar doing the work of a whole conversation."*

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- *Pensar + infinitivo* — useful but not essential; *ir a* covers near-future for A1
- Extended vocabulary for work/professional planning (*una conferencia, un viaje de negocios*)
- Conditional uses of *poder* (*podrías...*, *podría...*) — A2 territory
- *Deber* (moral obligation) — curriculum places this in Week 11 (*debes descansar* for doctor advice); don't introduce it here
- Complex sentences combining more than two structures

## Cultural Context for Instructors

- **Quedar:** The verb *quedar* (to arrange to meet) is extremely high-frequency in Spain: *"¿Quedamos el viernes?"* In Latin America, *verse* or *encontrarse* is more common. Flag the Spain/Latin America difference — both are worth knowing.
- **Planning informality in Spain and Latin America:** Spontaneous social plans are very common in many Spanish-speaking cultures — particularly Spain, Argentina, Mexico. Plans often solidify the day of or the day before. *"¿Nos vemos esta noche?"* is perfectly normal. This contrasts with the homework framing of planning in advance — make sure students know both registers.
- **Los planes:** Note that *planes* in Spanish means "plans" (social/personal plans), not "planes" (aircraft, which are *aviones*). A useful false friend flag.
- **Regional: *Coger* vs. *tomar*:** Students planning to say "I'm going to take the bus" should note that *voy a coger el autobús* (Spain) can be offensive in some Latin American varieties. For cross-regional audiences, use *voy a tomar el autobús* — universally neutral.
