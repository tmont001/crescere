# Spanish A1 — Week 8 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 15 and 16
**Theme:** Home, Neighborhood, and High-Frequency Irregular Verbs

---

## Week Objective

By the end of Week 8, students can:
- Use *hay / no hay* to describe what is (or isn't) in their home
- Name 8+ rooms and 8+ furniture/household items with correct gender
- Use prepositions of place (*encima de, debajo de, delante de, detrás de, entre, al lado de, al fondo de*) with *de + el = del* contractions
- Distinguish *hay* (existence) from *está/están* (location)
- Describe their home in 5–6 spoken sentences
- Conjugate and use *hacer, venir, decir, saber,* and *conocer* in context
- Distinguish *saber* from *conocer*
- Describe their neighborhood using adjectives and location phrases

**Relationship to the curriculum:** This is exactly the Week 8 content specified. Session 15 = home vocabulary + *hay* + prepositions. Session 16 = high-frequency irregular verbs + *saber vs. conocer* + neighborhood. No deferral from Week 7 is carried into this week.

**Note on *hay* vs. *estar*:** This distinction is worth explicit attention in Session 15. Students who have been using *hay* and *estar* interchangeably will produce persistent errors. The distinction (*hay* = existence, *está/están* = location of something known) is one of the most common sources of confusion at A1–A2 level.

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 8

| Mistake | Correction | When to address |
|---|---|---|
| *Hay el parque al lado del supermercado* (hay with a specific/known noun) | *El parque **está** al lado del supermercado.* When the thing is specific/known, use *estar* for location. *Hay* introduces new/unknown things. | Session 15 *hay* block |
| *No hay de garaje* (adding *de* before noun after no hay) | *No hay garaje.* After *no hay*, the article simply disappears — no additional *de* needed. (Unlike *ne...pas de* in French, Spanish *no hay* does not require *de*.) | Session 15 *hay* block |
| *Al lado de el pasillo* (not contracting) | *Al lado **del** pasillo.* *De + el = del* — mandatory. | Session 15 prepositions block |
| *Hay un sofá delante la televisión* (missing *de* in preposition) | *Delante **de** la televisión.* All these prepositions require *de* before the following noun. | Session 15 prepositions block |
| *Yo haces la compra* (using tú form for yo) | *Yo **hago** la compra.* Only *yo* form is irregular: *hago.* | Session 16 hacer block |
| *Vengo de trabajar mañana* (confusing venir de with time) | *Venir de + inf* = to have just done something (recent past). For future plans use *ir a + inf.* | Session 16 if it arises |
| *Sé a María* (saber with a person) | *Conozco a María.* People → *conocer*, with personal *a.* Facts/skills → *saber.* | Session 16 saber/conocer block |
| *Conozco que el tren llega tarde* (conocer with a que clause) | *Sé que el tren llega tarde.* Only *saber* can take a *que* clause. *Conocer* takes a noun: person or place. | Session 16 if it arises |
| Ser/estar with neighborhood adjectives | *Mi barrio **es** tranquilo* (permanent characteristic — ser). *Mi barrio **está** muy animado esta semana* (current/temporary — estar). Both natural in this context; continue to reinforce the distinction. | Session 16 neighborhood block |

## Suggested Instructor Language

- **For *hay* vs. *estar*:** *"'Hay' and 'está/están' are NOT interchangeable. 'Hay' says something exists — it's like announcing: 'There's a park near here.' 'Está' says where a specific, known thing is — 'The park is next to the supermarket.' Think of 'hay' as introducing something for the first time, and 'está' as locating something you both already know about."*

- **For *no hay*:** *"'No hay' is simpler than French 'il n'y a pas de' — there's no extra 'de' needed. Just 'no hay' plus the noun without any article: 'No hay garaje.' Not 'no hay un garaje' — drop the article after 'no hay.'"*

- **For *del* contraction:** *"Remember: 'a + el = al' from Week 5 (al banco, al supermercado). The same thing happens with 'de + el = del.' So: 'al lado del salón,' not 'al lado de el salón.' The contraction is mandatory in Spanish."*

- **For *hacer*:** *"'Hacer' is irregular only in yo: hago. All other forms are regular -er endings. This makes it easier than it looks. The most useful expressions with hacer: 'hacer la compra' (grocery shopping), 'hacer deporte' (exercise), 'hacer una pregunta' (ask a question), and weather: 'hace frío/calor/sol.'"*

- **For *saber* vs. *conocer*:** *"The quick rule: 'Saber' is for knowing facts and skills — things you can verify, look up, or demonstrate. 'Conocer' is for knowing people and places — things you've personally encountered. And only 'saber' can take a 'que' clause: 'Sé que mañana hay clases.' You can't say 'conozco que...' — ever."*

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- *Decir* extended uses (*¿Qué me dices?*, indirect speech) — just *¿Cómo se dice...?* and one or two sentence examples
- More than 8–10 rooms and 8–10 furniture items — quality over quantity
- Full regional vocabulary variants — mention one (e.g., *salón* Spain / *sala* Latin America) but do not run through exhaustive lists
- *Venir de + infinitivo* (to have just done something) — save for A2 review
- Extended neighborhood adjectives beyond 4–5 core ones

## Cultural Context for Instructors

- **Housing vocabulary — regional:** *El piso* (Spain) / *el apartamento, el departamento* (Latin America). *La nevera* (Spain, colloquial) / *el frigorífico* (Spain, formal) / *el refrigerador* (Latin America). Students should know one variant from each pair; both are widely understood.
- **Spanish apartment sizes:** Similar to French — Spaniards describe apartments by number of *habitaciones* (bedrooms). A *piso de dos habitaciones* has two bedrooms (the salon is not counted). *Un estudio* = one main room. *Un ático* in Spain often refers to a top-floor apartment with a terrace — not an attic.
- **El barrio:** Neighborhood identity is very strong in Spanish and Latin American cities. In Buenos Aires, neighborhoods (*barrios*) have distinct personalities (La Boca, Palermo, San Telmo). In Madrid, *el Barrio de las Letras, la Latina, Malasaña.* In Mexico City, *Condesa, Roma, Coyoacán.* Students who know their target city can personalize the speaking round.
