# Spanish A1 — Week 5 Student Overview

**Theme: Tener, Age, and Family**

---

## What You Will Learn This Week

Week 5 introduces *tener* — to have. It is the third essential Spanish verb after *ser* and *estar*, and it works differently than you might expect.

In Spanish, age is something you **have**, not something you **are.** Hunger, thirst, cold, and sleepiness are also things you have. This week you will add *tener* to your toolkit and use it to describe yourself and the people around you.

You will also learn a core set of family vocabulary and how to combine *tener + ser + estar + regular verbs* to give a real description of someone.

---

## What You Will Be Able to Say by the End of Week 5

By the end of Session 10, you should be able to describe your family or the people closest to you:

> *Tengo una hermana y un hermano. Mi hermana se llama Ana — tiene treinta y ocho años. Es médica y trabaja en un hospital en Valencia. Está muy ocupada pero es muy paciente. Mi hermano tiene cuarenta y dos años. Tiene dos hijos. Vive en el extranjero y trabaja en tecnología. Mis padres tienen setenta años y están jubilados. Viven en el pueblo donde nacimos.*

And answer spontaneously:
- *¿Cuántos años tienes?* — *Tengo [X] años.*
- *¿Tienes hermanos?* — *Sí, tengo [X]. / No, soy hijo/a único/a.*
- *¿Cuántos años tiene tu madre?* — *Tiene [X] años.*

## Tener — The Essential Conjugation

| Subject | Tener |
|---|---|
| yo | **tengo** |
| tú | tienes |
| él / ella / usted | tiene |
| nosotros/as | tenemos |
| vosotros/as | tenéis |
| ellos / ellas / ustedes | tienen |

**The only irregular form is *yo*: tengo.** All other forms follow the regular -er pattern you already know. After this week, *tengo* will be as automatic as *soy* and *estoy.*

---

## Age in Spanish

This surprises almost every beginner:

- English: I **am** thirty.
- Spanish: **Tengo** treinta años. (I **have** thirty years.)

*Soy treinta años* is wrong in Spanish. Age always uses *tener.* This parallels the French pattern (*J'ai trente ans*) — the same logic.

---

## Tener Expressions — Things You Have

Spanish uses *tener* where English uses "to be" for physical feelings:

| Spanish | English |
|---|---|
| Tengo hambre. | I'm hungry. |
| Tengo sed. | I'm thirsty. |
| Tengo sueño. | I'm sleepy. |
| Tengo frío. | I'm cold. |
| Tengo razón. | I'm right. |

The key: **do not use *estar* for these.** *Estoy hambre* is incorrect. *Tengo hambre* — you have hunger.

---

## Possessives: Mi, Tu, Su

To describe your family you need three possessive words:

| Spanish | Meaning | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|---|
| mi / mis | my | *mi hermano* | *mis hermanos* |
| tu / tus | your | *tu madre* | *tus padres* |
| su / sus | his / her / their | *su hijo* | *sus hijos* |

**Important:** Possessives agree with the *noun*, not with you. Whether you are male or female, you say *mi hermana* for your sister and *mis hermanas* for your sisters.

## What to Practice Between Sessions

**After Session 9 — before Session 10:**

1. **Say the tener conjugation from memory.** *Tengo, tienes, tiene, tenemos, tenéis, tienen.* Especially *tengo* — say it until it is automatic.
2. **Write your age and 3 family sentences.** *Tengo X años. Tengo [X] hermanos. Mi hermano tiene [X] años.*
3. **Say them out loud.** No notes on the third try.

**After Session 10 — before Session 11:**

1. **Write your family description paragraph.** 5–7 sentences. Mix *tener, ser, estar,* and at least one regular verb.
2. **Say it without notes.** Third try with no looking.
3. **Add someone outside family.** 2–3 sentences describing a close friend or colleague using the same combination of verbs.
