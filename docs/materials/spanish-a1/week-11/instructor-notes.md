# Spanish A1 — Week 11 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 21 and 22
**Theme:** Health and the Body

---

## Week Objective

By the end of Week 11, students can:
- Name at least 12 body parts with the correct definite article
- Use *doler* correctly — *me duele* (singular) / *me duelen* (plural) — with the definite article on the body part
- Describe symptoms in a pharmacy context (*tengo fiebre, estoy resfriado/a, me duele el estómago*)
- Ask a pharmacist for a product (*¿Tiene algo para...? / Necesito algo para el resfriado*)
- Give or receive advice using *deber + infinitivo* and *deberías + infinitivo*
- Express duration of symptoms using *llevar + time + gerund / con*
- Handle a basic doctor's appointment role play end-to-end

**Relationship to the curriculum:** This is exactly the Week 11 content specified in the curriculum. Session 21 = body parts + *doler* structure + pharmacy scenario. Session 22 = *deber + infinitivo*, *deberías* (as formula), *llevar + time + gerund*, doctor's appointment role play.

**Prior knowledge students bring:**
- *Gustar* structure (Week 6 — food preferences, Week 10 — hobbies): *doler* is the same structure. **Make this connection explicit and early.**
- *Encantar* (Week 10 — same structure as *gustar*): another reinforcement of the structure.
- *Estar* for states (Week 2 — *estoy cansado/a, estoy enfermo/a*): symptoms use *estar* — *estoy resfriado/a*.
- *Tener* expressions (Week 4 — *tener hambre, tener frío*): *tener fiebre* follows the same pattern.
- *Quisiera / querer* (Weeks 6, 12): *quiero/quisiera algo para...* is available if students want a polite opener at the pharmacy.

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 11

| Mistake | Correction | When to address |
|---|---|---|
| *Me duele mi cabeza* (possessive instead of definite article) | *Me duele **la** cabeza.* — Spanish always uses the definite article with body parts in pain structures. | Session 21, *doler* block — address immediately |
| *Me duelo la cabeza* (conjugating like a regular verb) | *Me **duele** la cabeza.* — *Doler* works like *gustar*: the body part is the subject; the verb agrees with the body part. | Session 21, *doler* block |
| *Me gusta la cabeza* (using *gustar* instead of *doler*) | *Me **duele** la cabeza.* — Connect them: *gustar* = likes; *doler* = hurts. Same structure, different verb. | Session 21 if it arises |
| *Me duelen la cabeza* (wrong agreement — singular noun) | *Me **duele** la cabeza.* — Singular subject → *duele.* It is only *duelen* when the noun is plural. | Session 21, *doler* drill |
| *Tengo la fiebre* (adding article to *fiebre*) | *Tengo fiebre.* — No article after *tener* in fixed expressions (*tener hambre, tener fiebre, tener frío*). | Session 21, symptoms block |
| *Estoy resfriada de un día* (using *de* for duration) | *Llevo un día resfriada.* OR *Estoy resfriada desde ayer.* — *Llevar* is the standard structure for ongoing duration in Spanish. | Session 22, *llevar* block |
| *Llevo tres días tosiendo*: gerund formed as *tosando* | *Tosiendo* — *toser* is an -er verb; gerund is *-iendo*, not *-ando*. | Session 22, *llevar* block |
| *Deberías de descansar* (adding *de*) | *Deberías descansar.* — No preposition between *deber/deberías* and the infinitive. | Session 22, *deber* block |

## Suggested Instructor Language

**For connecting *doler* to *gustar*:**
*"You already know gustar — me gusta el fútbol. The sport is the subject; the verb agrees with the sport. Doler works exactly the same. The body part is the subject; the verb agrees with the body part. Me duele la cabeza — la cabeza is singular, so duele. Me duelen los pies — los pies is plural, so duelen. Same structure. Two verbs, same logic."*

**For the definite article with body parts:**
*"In English you say 'my head hurts.' In Spanish, you don't say 'mi cabeza' — you say 'me duele la cabeza.' The 'me' already tells us it's your head. The body part gets the definite article, not a possessive. This is exactly the same as gustar — me gusta el fútbol, not me gusta mi fútbol."*

**For *tener fiebre* without article:**
*"Tener fiebre follows the same pattern as tener hambre, tener sed, tener miedo — all the tener expressions from Week 4. No article. Tengo fiebre, not tengo la fiebre."*

**For *llevar + time + gerund*:**
*"Llevar is the verb for duration in Spanish. 'Llevo tres días tosiendo' — I've been coughing for three days. Think of it like 'I carry three days of coughing.' The gerund (-ando/-iendo) describes what you've been doing. If you can't form the gerund, use the con construction: llevo tres días con fiebre, llevo una semana con esta tos. Both are correct."*

**For *deberías* as formula:**
*"Deberías is the conditional of deber — 'you would/should.' Don't worry about the full conditional form yet — just learn deberías as a formula for giving polite advice. A doctor would say deberías beber más agua — you should drink more water. More polite than debes. Use either in the role play."*

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- *El oído* vs. *la oreja* distinction (inner vs. outer ear) — both mean "ear" at A1 level; use *la oreja* as default
- *Deberías* distinction from *debes* in depth — both are acceptable; *deberías* as formula is sufficient
- Extended *llevar* uses beyond health contexts (*llevo dos horas esperando*) — stay in health domain for Week 11
- Medical appointment booking vocabulary (*pedir cita por teléfono*) — extension only
- Extended body part vocabulary beyond the core 12
- Regional vocabulary for healthcare (*el consultorio, la clínica, el seguro médico*) — mention *consultorio* vs. *clínica* briefly if cohort is interested

## Preparing Students for the Week 12 Final Performance Task

Week 12 Part A (spoken role play, 5–7 min) involves: meeting someone, exchanging personal information (ser/estar), making a plan (ir a + infinitivo), giving directions. Health is not explicitly required for Part A, per the curriculum's final performance description.

However, Week 11 provides important preparation:
1. **Role play stamina:** Students who can navigate a full pharmacy and doctor encounter without notes are more confident entering Week 12 extended scenarios.
2. **Estar for conditions:** Students revisit *estoy resfriado/a, estoy mareado/a, estoy cansado/a* — reinforcing the ser/estar distinction from Week 2 in a practical context.
3. **Llevar** can appear naturally in Week 12 self-description: *Llevo tres meses estudiando español* — I've been studying Spanish for three months. Flag this connection explicitly at the Session 22 wrap-up.

Week 12 Part B (written message, 8–10 sentences): introduce yourself, describe routine, suggest a plan. Health vocabulary is not required, but *llevar* for duration is a high-value addition students can deploy.

## Cultural Context for Instructors

**Spanish healthcare system:** Spain has a strong public healthcare system (Sistema Nacional de Salud). In Latin America, systems vary widely — private clinics (*clínicas*) are common alongside public hospitals. Students from Latin American countries may have different healthcare vocabulary (*el médico, el doctor, el consultorio, el seguro*).

**Pharmacist role:** In Spain, pharmacists (farmacéuticos) are highly trained and commonly consulted for minor ailments before visiting a doctor — similar to France. *"¿Qué me recomienda?"* (What do you recommend?) is a natural phrase in a Spanish pharmacy.

**Doler in natural speech:** *Me duele mucho la espalda* is one of the most common sentences in everyday Spanish conversation. The structure appears in non-medical contexts too: *Me duele verlos sufrir* (it pains me to see them suffer). Connecting it to *gustar* and pointing out its frequency outside health contexts builds student confidence.

**Regional vocabulary note:** *Tirita* (plaster/bandage) is Spain-specific; *curita* is common in Latin America; *vendita* or *cura* in other regions. Mention in passing — do not drill regional variants in the role play.
