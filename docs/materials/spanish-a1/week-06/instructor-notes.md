# Spanish A1 — Week 6 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 11 and 12
**Theme:** Food, Preferences, and Restaurants

---

## Week Objective

By the end of Week 6, students can:
- Use the *gustar* structure correctly (*me gusta / me gustan*) with food vocabulary and infinitives
- Conjugate *querer* (e→ie stem change) and use *quisiera* as a polite ordering formula
- Name and discuss 8–10 Spanish and Latin American food items
- Navigate a full restaurant interaction in Spanish: ordering, asking questions, requesting the bill
- Complete a 3–4 minute café or restaurant role play with a partner
- Explain food preferences using *gustar, encantar,* and *no me gusta*

**Relationship to the curriculum:** This is exactly the Week 6 content specified in the curriculum. Session 11 is the *gustar* structure + food vocabulary; Session 12 is *querer/quisiera* + full restaurant role play. No deferral from Week 5 is carried into this week — tener/family content is now closed.

**Gustar is the grammatical challenge of the week.** The curriculum flags it explicitly: "English speakers instinctively want to say *yo gusto el café.* The indirect object (me, te, le...) + gusta/gustan structure is counterintuitive. Spend real time here; errors persist without it." Plan extra time for the initial explanation and first drilling block.

**Possessives note:** *Nuestro/vuestro* was listed as extension in Week 5 materials. The curriculum does not assign them to Week 6 either. If students ask, point them to the vocabulary file. Do not drill them this week.

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 6

| Mistake | Correction | When to address |
|---|---|---|
| *Yo gusto el café* (wrong structure) | *Me gusta el café.* The indirect object pronoun (*me*) goes before the verb; the food is the subject. Explicit correction every time. | Session 11 gustar block |
| *Me gusta los tacos* (singular verb with plural subject) | *Me **gustan** los tacos.* The verb must agree with the noun (the thing liked), not with you. | Session 11 gusta/gustan drill |
| *Me gusta mucho los tacos* (word order with mucho) | *Me gustan **mucho** los tacos.* Word order: [pronoun] + [gustan] + [noun] + [mucho]. *Mucho* follows the noun. | Session 11 if it arises |
| *Quero una tortilla* (dropping the stem change) | *Qui**e**ro una tortilla.* The e→ie change applies to all forms except nosotros/vosotros. | Session 12 conjugation block |
| *¿La cuenta, por favor?* (with interrogative intonation unnecessarily) | *La cuenta, por favor.* is a statement, not a question. Just say it directly and politely. | Session 12 role play |
| *Soy alérgico al…* (wrong preposition form) | *al* = *a + el* (article contraction). *Soy alérgico a la nuez.* vs. *Soy alérgico **al** trigo* (el trigo). | Session 12 if it arises |
| Ser/estar errors in food context | *Este plato está muy rico.* (estar — current state). *Esta cocina es buena.* (ser — permanent quality). Continue to correct; food context gives natural opportunities. | Every session |
| *Me encanta los tacos* (singular verb with plural) | *Me **encantan** los tacos.* Encantar follows the same rule as gustar. | Session 11 encantar block |

## Suggested Instructor Language

- **For introducing gustar:** *"Gustar works backwards from English. In English: 'I like coffee' — I is the subject. In Spanish: 'Me gusta el café' — the coffee is the subject. The coffee pleases me. So the verb agrees with the coffee, not with you. This means: 'Me gusta el café' (coffee, singular) but 'Me gustan los tacos' (tacos, plural). The pronoun in front — me, te, le — tells us who is being pleased. It never changes for singular/plural."*

- **For gusta vs. gustan:** *"Ask yourself: what is the thing you like? Is it singular or plural? If singular: gusta. If plural: gustan. If you like doing something (an infinitive), always use gusta: 'Me gusta cocinar.' If you like a group of things: 'Me gustan las películas de acción.'"*

- **For quisiera:** *"'Quisiera' is the magic word in restaurants. Literally it's a past subjunctive — we won't study that until much later. For now, treat it as a set phrase that means 'I would like.' It's softer and more polite than 'quiero.' Both are correct; 'quisiera' is the safer choice in formal settings."*

- **For the role play:** *"In a Spanish restaurant, the waiter often comes to the table and asks '¿Qué van a tomar?' (What will you have?) or '¿Qué les pongo?' (What shall I get you?). You answer 'Para mí...' or 'Quisiera...' When you're ready for the bill: 'La cuenta, por favor.' In Spain, calling the waiter with '¡Oiga!' (Excuse me!) is acceptable; in Latin America, 'Disculpe' is more common."*

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- Full *gustar* with all six indirect object pronouns — *me/te/le* are enough for Session 11; *nos/os/les* can be introduced as reference
- *Encantar* drilling — show two examples; do not spend more than 3 minutes on it
- More than 10 food vocabulary items — quality over quantity; students need to say the words aloud, not recognize a list
- Tipping norms across all countries — one or two concrete examples are enough
- Stem-changing verbs as a category — introduce *quiero* as part of *querer*; do not launch a full lesson on e→ie verbs this week

## Cultural Context for Instructors

- **El menú del día (Spain):** A fixed-price lunch menu (typically €10–15), usually including two courses, bread, and a drink. Very common in Spanish restaurants. Students visiting Spain will encounter it immediately.
- **Tipping:** In Spain, tipping is appreciated but not mandatory — 5–10% for good service is common. In Mexico, 10–15% is standard. In Argentina, tipping is common (10%). Service is rarely included automatically.
- **Regional staff titles:** *El camarero / la camarera* (Spain); *el mesero / la mesera* (Mexico, Colombia, most of Latin America); *el mozo / la moza* (Argentina/Uruguay). Students should know the most common one for their target region.
- **Dietary vocabulary:** *Sin gluten* (gluten-free), *sin lactosa* (dairy-free), *vegetariano/a* (vegetarian), *vegano/a* (vegan) — these are increasingly well understood in Spanish restaurants.
- **Drink culture:** In Spain, drinks often arrive with free tapas in some regions (Granada, Salamanca, Almería). In Mexico and Latin America, *agua de frutas* (fruit water) is a staple. Students should know that *un café* in Spain = espresso; if they want American-style drip coffee, they ask for *café americano*.
