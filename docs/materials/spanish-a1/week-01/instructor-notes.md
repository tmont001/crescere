# Spanish A1 — Week 1 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 1 and 2
**Theme:** Sounds, Greetings, and First Introductions with Ser
**Week objective:** Students can greet someone in Spanish, introduce themselves using *ser*, ask 2–3 questions, and hold a short back-and-forth exchange. They leave Session 2 having spoken Spanish out loud to the group.

---

## What Must Be Protected in Live Class Time

- **The five pure Spanish vowels.** English speakers have been mispronouncing these their entire lives. 7–8 minutes of live modeling and repetition in Session 1 is the single best pronunciation investment you can make. Do not skip it.
- **Ser — spoken production.** Every student needs to say all six forms out loud with correction before they leave Session 1. Reading a conjugation table is not the same as producing the forms under pressure.
- **Tú vs. usted — concrete rules.** Students will default to *usted* because it feels polite. They need permission and clear examples to use *tú* with peers.
- **The complete introduction, spoken aloud.** Every student must give a full Spanish introduction before the end of Session 2. Build time for this — it is the week's main milestone.
- **Ser vs. estar firewall.** Do not distinguish *ser* from *estar* in Week 1. If a student asks, say: "That is the most important question in Spanish grammar and it is all of Week 2. Hold it."

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 1

| Mistake | Notes |
|---|---|
| Vowels with English glides | *no* sounds like "noh" instead of a clean *o*. Correct gently and consistently throughout Week 1. |
| *Yo soy llamo...* (mixing *me llamo* with *soy*) | Teach *me llamo* as a fixed phrase. "We'll explain the grammar in Week 3." |
| No gender agreement on nationality: *soy americano* (female speaker) | Flag in Session 2 without teaching the full agreement system. Students often self-correct when they hear it. |
| Using *usted* with everyone | Normal beginner overcorrection. Reassure them: *tú* is right for this class and for most peer interactions. |
| Confusing *soy* (permanent) with *estoy* (state) | Do not address this in Week 1 — it is the entire topic of Week 2. If a student raises it: "Great question. That is all of Week 2. Hold it." |
| Forgetting that *vosotros* is Spain-only | If the cohort is Latin America-focused, acknowledge *vosotros* in the table but use *ustedes* as the working default. |

---

## Suggested Instructor Language

- **For vowels:** *"In English the letter 'a' sounds like 'ay.' In Spanish, a always sounds like 'ah' — one sound, no glide, every time. Let's practice."*
- **For ser:** *"This is the most important verb in Spanish this week. By the end of tonight you will know all six forms. I am going to hold you to that."*
- **For me llamo:** *"Don't ask why it works this way yet. Just know the phrase — me llamo [name]. We'll explain the grammar in Week 3."*
- **For tú/usted:** *"If you'd use someone's first name in English, use tú. If you'd say sir or ma'am, use usted. In this class, use tú with each other."*
- **For the full introduction:** *"You now know enough to introduce yourself in Spanish. I want each of you to do it — notes are fine, but say the words out loud."*

---

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- The alphabet (Session 1 homework — very close to English; students can learn it independently)
- *c/z* regional variation — mention it once if it comes up; do not drill it in Week 1
- Syllable stress rules — point to the reference in the vocabulary list; move on
- Extended nationality/profession vocabulary — give them the list; drill only what is relevant to the room
- *Vosotros* extended practice — show it in the table; use *ustedes* as the working form

## What to Assign as Homework If Pacing Runs Long

If Session 1 runs out of time before numbers:
→ Numbers 1–20 are in the Session 1 homework. Assign them explicitly.

If Session 2 runs out of time before the introduction round:
→ Ask each student to record a 30-second voice introduction and share it before Session 3. Not ideal — the group moment matters — but better than skipping it entirely.

Both homework files are pre-written and ready to distribute.

---

## Note: Ser vs. Estar — Week 1 Firewall

Do not distinguish *ser* from *estar* in Week 1. Students will encounter *estar* in Week 2 as its own topic. For now, *soy/eres/es...* means "am/are/is" — full stop.

The greetings contain *¿Cómo estás?* — teach it as a fixed social phrase. Do not explain why it uses *estar*. If students ask, say: "Week 2."

Do not let a curious student pull the ser/estar distinction forward into Week 1. Week 2 is structured specifically to build this understanding correctly, starting from scratch.

---

## Week 1 Priority Reminder

Every student should speak Spanish in both sessions. The goal is not accuracy — it is participation. Students who stay quiet in Week 1 develop a habit that is hard to break. Correct fast, keep the energy high, do not linger on errors. End every session with a moment where students feel they did something real.
