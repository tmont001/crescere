# Spanish A1 — Session 1 Slide Outline
## Sounds, Greetings, and Ser

**Session length:** 60 minutes
**Session-end goal:** Students can greet someone in Spanish, give their name and where they are from using *soy*, recognize all six forms of *ser*, and begin counting to 20. Full production of the complete conjugation is reinforced through homework and confirmed in Session 2.

> **Pacing note:** Pronunciation is worth protecting — but awareness, not drilling. If you sense the group needs more time on *ser* or the speaking activity, shorten the consonant slide before cutting anything else.

---

## Core 60-Minute Plan

### Slide 1 — Welcome (5 min)
**Purpose:** Orient students; set the tone for a speaking-first course.
**Teacher notes:** Introduce yourself. Explain the format: live, small group, speaking starts today. Say: "Session 1 will feel like a lot of new things at once. That is normal. You do not need to master anything tonight — you just need to start."
**Student activity:** None — instructor speaking.

---

### Slide 2 — The Five Spanish Vowels (8 min)
**Purpose:** Lock in the most important pronunciation habit before students start speaking.
**Teacher notes:**
- Spanish has five vowels. Each has exactly one sound — always, no exceptions, no glides.
  - **a** — "ah": *casa, hablar, mañana*
  - **e** — "eh": *mesa, ser, ese*
  - **i** — "ee": *sí, vivir, diez*
  - **o** — "oh" (no "ow" glide): *no, como, solo*
  - **u** — "oo": *tú, mucho, usted*
- The English trap: English adds a glide to vowels. Say "no" in English — it ends in a slight "w." Spanish *no* cuts it off clean. Demonstrate both and let students hear the difference.
- Drill: model each vowel → all students repeat → say simple words containing only those vowels → students repeat.
- Say: "These five sounds are the foundation of your Spanish accent. We are not going to master them tonight — but we are going to start noticing them."
**Student activity:** Choral repetition of each vowel and practice words.

---

### Slide 3 — How Spanish Consonants Sound: The Big Four (5 min)
**Purpose:** Awareness only — students need to hear these before they start speaking.
**Teacher notes:** Name and demonstrate each once. Students listen and try once. No drilling.
- **r (single tap):** Like the *d* or *t* in "butter" in American English. *pero, caro, para.* They can approximate this.
- **rr (trill):** Tongue tip vibrates. *perro, Roberto.* Say: "This one takes time. A good approximation is fine in Week 1."
- **j (jota):** A strong h-sound from the back of the throat. *jefe, Juan, ojo.*
- **ñ:** Nasal y-sound — its own letter in Spanish. *mañana, español, año.*
- Mention *h* is always silent (*hola, hablar*) — no special practice needed.
**Student activity:** Listen and try each sound once. No individual correction yet.

---

### Slide 4 — Greetings: Formal and Informal (8 min)
**Purpose:** Students can greet, respond, and say goodbye in Spanish.
**Teacher notes:**
- Core greetings: *Hola* (universal), *Buenos días* (morning), *Buenas tardes* (afternoon), *Buenas noches* (evening), *Buenas* (casual shortcut, any time).
- Core leave-takings: *Adiós, Hasta luego, Hasta mañana, Hasta pronto.*
- Social exchange: *¿Cómo estás?* → *Bien, gracias. ¿Y tú?* — teach as a fixed phrase. Do not explain *estar* yet.
- *Mucho gusto / Encantado/a* — nice to meet you. Flag the -a ending for women without explaining agreement rules.
- Choral repetition. Then pairs greet each other — one round formal, one informal.
**Student activity:** Choral repetition → pair greetings (2 rounds).

---

### Slide 5 — Tú vs. Usted (5 min)
**Purpose:** Students know which register to use before they start speaking.
**Teacher notes:**
- *Tú* = first-name basis. *Usted* = title basis, professional/formal context.
- In this class: *tú* with each other.
- Common beginner error: everyone defaults to *usted* because it feels polite. Correct this: "In a class with your peers, *tú* is the right word. Using *usted* with classmates sounds odd — like calling a friend 'sir.'"
- Regional note: In Colombia and Costa Rica, *usted* is used even with family members. Mention it so students are not confused if they encounter it.
- Quick scenarios: (a) your classmate → *tú*; (b) a manager you are meeting → *usted*; (c) your grandmother's neighbor → *usted*.
**Student activity:** 3 scenarios — students call out *tú* or *usted*.

---

### Slide 6 — Ser: Present Tense (8 min)
**Purpose:** Students see and hear all six forms of *ser*, begin to recognize them, and leave with *soy* as a usable form. Full production of the table is homework and Session 2.
**Teacher notes:**
- Display the full table. Say each form, students repeat — twice through. The goal is recognition and first exposure, not instant mastery.
- Pronunciation notes: *soy* (oi diphthong); *eres* (clean e sounds); *somos/son* (clean o); *sois* (Spain only — explain this).
- Vosotros note: show it in the table. "If your Spanish is Latin American-oriented, you'll use *ustedes son* — not *vosotros sois.* I'll note these regional distinctions as they come up."
- Do NOT introduce *estar*. If a student asks "How is this different from *estar*?" — say: "Great question. That is all of Week 2. Hold it."
- After the table: each student says *Yo soy [name].* Instructor uses *soy de* and *eres de* naturally in context. Do not pressure-test the full table at this point — that is what Session 2 warm-up and homework are for.

| Subject | Ser |
|---|---|
| yo | soy |
| tú | eres |
| él / ella / usted | es |
| nosotros/as | somos |
| vosotros/as | sois (Spain) |
| ellos / ellas / ustedes | son |

**Student activity:** Choral conjugation × 2 → each student says *Yo soy [name]* → instructor uses 1–2 other forms naturally in context (no individual pressure-test).

---

### Slide 7 — Me Llamo and First Phrases (8 min)
**Purpose:** Students can give a basic three-sentence introduction.
**Teacher notes:**
- Teach *me llamo* as a fixed phrase. Do not explain reflexive verbs. "We'll cover why this works this way in Week 3. For now, just know it."
- The four core phrases:
  - *Me llamo [name].* — My name is... (fixed phrase)
  - *Soy de [city/country].* — I'm from...
  - *Soy [profession].* — I'm a [profession]. **No article** — contrast with English "I am a doctor."
  - *Soy [nationality].* — I'm [nationality]. Flag gender agreement: *soy americano* / *soy americana.*
- Most common error to catch immediately: *Yo soy llamo* (mixing *soy* with *me llamo*). Correct it the first time.
- Model your own complete introduction. Then each student repeats using their own information.
**Student activity:** Instructor models → students give their own three-sentence introduction to a partner → one volunteer to the group.

---

### Slide 8 — Numbers 1–20 (7 min)
**Purpose:** Students can count to 20 and recognize spoken numbers.
**Teacher notes:**
- Count 1–20 together. Say each number, students repeat.
- Pronunciation notes as you go: *uno* (clean o); *tres* (the r); *cuatro* (qu = kw + the r); *diez* (diphthong + z).
- 11–15 are irregular (once, doce, trece, catorce, quince) — must be memorized.
- 16–19 follow a pattern: dieciséis, diecisiete, dieciocho, diecinueve.
- Count together × 2. Then spot-drill: call random numbers 1–20.
**Student activity:** Choral count × 2 → random number drill.

---

### Slide 9 — Wrap-Up (2 min)
**Purpose:** Set clear homework expectations; close positively.
**Teacher notes:** Say: "You spoke Spanish tonight. That is a real thing. The homework has four short parts — work through the ser table until you can say it from memory, count to 30, and write four sentences. You will not be tested on the full conjugation tonight — that is what Session 2 is for."
**Student activity:** Students note homework. Optional: one word in Spanish each before logging off.

---

## Cut First If Running Behind

Cut in this order — most dispensable first:

1. **Slide 3 (consonants):** Skip entirely if you are running behind after the vowels slide. Say: "There are some other sounds in Spanish — they're in your homework reference and we'll keep circling back. The vowels are the most important thing tonight."
2. **Slide 8 (numbers):** Reduce to 1–10 live and assign 11–20 as homework extension.
3. **Slide 5 (tú/usted scenarios):** Cut the scenarios; just state the rule and move on.
4. Do not cut Slides 6 or 7 — students must produce *ser* forms and an introduction before they leave.

---

## Extension for Fast Cohorts

Only if you have time remaining:

- **Alphabet (8 min):** Teach Spanish letter names. Most useful early: *j* (jota), *ñ* (eñe), *ll* (elle), *rr* (doble erre). Ask each student to spell their first name.
- **Numbers 21–30 (3 min):** Teach veintiuno through treinta. Count together.
- **Introduction with nationality (4 min):** Add *Soy [nationality]* to the introduction and drill gender agreement briefly.

---

## Move to Homework If Needed

- Regional pronunciation variation for *c/z* (Spain vs. Latin America)
- Syllable stress rules (reference in vocabulary list)
- Detailed consonant drilling beyond the four covered in Slide 3
