# French A1 — Week 9 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 17 and 18
**Theme:** Plans and Obligations

---

## Week Objective

By the end of Week 9, students can:
- Use *aller + infinitif* to express near-future plans in speech
- Use common future time expressions fluently (*demain, ce soir, la semaine prochaine, dans deux jours*)
- Conjugate and use *devoir + infinitif* to express obligation
- Use *vouloir + infinitif* and *pouvoir + infinitif* in planning contexts (both reviewed from Week 6)
- Combine all three modals (*devoir, vouloir, pouvoir*) in real scheduling conversations
- Conduct a short planning role play: suggesting, accepting, declining, and negotiating times

**Relationship to the curriculum:** This is exactly the Week 9 content specified in the curriculum. Session 17 = *aller + infinitif* + future time expressions. Session 18 = *devoir/vouloir/pouvoir* + combined planning scenarios.

**Prior knowledge students bring:** *Aller* conjugation (Week 5), *vouloir* and *pouvoir* conjugations (Week 6), *il faut* (introduced implicitly in Week 7 and 8 materials, though not drilled). *Devoir* is genuinely new.

**What changes from Week 6 to Week 9 for *vouloir/pouvoir*:** In Week 6, students used *je voudrais* (polite conditional) for restaurant ordering and *pouvoir* briefly (*je peux essayer?*). In Week 9, both are used as full modals — students encounter *je veux/il veut/ils veulent* and *je peux/il peut/ils peuvent* in combination patterns for the first time.

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 9

| Mistake | Correction | When to address |
|---|---|---|
| *Je vais à aller* (using *aller* twice) | *Je vais aller au cinéma.* Only ONE *aller* — the first conjugated; the second is the infinitive of the main verb. | Session 17 immediately |
| *Je vais travailler demain soir ce week-end* (piling time expressions) | One clear time expression per sentence at A1. *Je vais travailler demain soir. Ce week-end, je vais me reposer.* | If it arises in session 17 |
| *Je dois partir maintenant* vs. *Je vais partir maintenant* | Both are grammatically fine; they differ in meaning. *Je dois* = I must (obligation). *Je vais* = I'm going to (neutral future). Neither is wrong — the choice depends on whether there's an obligation behind the action. | Session 18 devoir block |
| *Je veux aller mais je ne peut pas* (wrong person) | *Je ne peux pas.* Yo form is *peux*, not *peut* (*peut* is il/elle). | Session 18 pouvoir review |
| *Ils voulivent* (invented form) | *Ils veulent.* Completely irregular — the -eu- disappears in plural. | Session 18 vouloir review |
| *Je dois de travailler* (adding *de*) | *Je dois travailler.* No preposition between modal and infinitive in French. Same for *vouloir* and *pouvoir.* | Session 18 if it arises |
| *La semaine prochain* (gender error) | *La semaine prochaine* — *semaine* is feminine; the adjective must agree. | Session 17 time expressions block |

## Suggested Instructor Language

- **For *aller + infinitif*:** *"You already know 'je vais au cinéma' — going to a place. Now we add an action after it: 'je vais voir un film.' Same aller, same conjugation you learned in Week 5. The only new part is what comes after. 'Je vais + infinitive' is the easiest way to talk about future plans in French — it's what native speakers use in everyday conversation far more than the grammatical future tense."*

- **For the negation of *aller + infinitif*:** *"Negation wraps around 'aller': 'Je ne vais pas travailler demain.' The infinitive stays outside. Same pattern as 'Je ne vais pas au cinéma' — you just extend it."*

- **For *devoir*:** *"Devoir is the verb for 'I have to' or 'I must.' Think of it as a verb of obligation — something you need to do, maybe because of work, an appointment, or a commitment. Je dois travailler — I have to work. Tu dois appeler — You need to call. The key irregular forms: 'vous devez' (not 'devez-vous' in isolation) and 'ils doivent' — the 'oi' sound in most forms."*

- **For distinguishing the three modals:** *"Three verbs — three meanings: 'Je dois' = I have to (it's an obligation). 'Je veux' = I want to (it's my desire). 'Je peux' = I can (it's possible / I'm able to). In real planning conversations, you'll use all three in the same exchange: 'Je veux venir, mais je dois travailler. Demain, je peux.'"*

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- *Penser + infinitif* (intention, softer than *aller + inf*) — not in the French A1 curriculum explicitly; use only with fast cohorts
- Extended work scheduling vocabulary (*une conférence, un déplacement*) — keep it to everyday events
- Conditional uses of *devoir* (*vous devriez...*) — this is A2 territory
- *Falloir* (*il faut* in full) — students may have encountered it informally; do not teach the full paradigm until Week 11 (curriculum places *il faut + infinitif* there)
- Complex sentence combining more than two modals in one clause

## Cultural Context for Instructors

- **French social planning:** French social culture tends toward slightly more formal advance planning than some Anglo cultures. *"On se retrouve demain soir"* is fine; spontaneous plans are common among close friends but professional and family plans are typically made further in advance. *Rendez-vous* has a wider meaning in French than English "rendezvous" — it covers any appointment or meeting, from a doctor to a dinner.
- **Calendar language in France:** Week numbers are used in professional contexts (*"la semaine 23"*). For A1 students, day+date is most practical: *"Je vais arriver le jeudi 5 juin."* The 24-hour clock is standard in formal contexts; students should recognize both.
- **Vous vs. tu in planning:** When inviting someone, the register matters. *"Vous êtes libre demain?"* (formal) vs. *"Tu es libre demain?"* (informal). At A1 level, students should practice both forms.
