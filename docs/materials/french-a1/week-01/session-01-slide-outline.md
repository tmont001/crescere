# French A1 — Session 1 Slide Outline
## Sounds, Greetings, and First Words

**Session length:** 60 minutes
**Session-end goal:** Students can greet someone in French, give their name and where they are from, say *je suis*, use *tu* vs. *vous* correctly, and count to at least 20.

> **Pacing note:** The session is designed to feel unhurried. If a block takes longer because students need repetition — let it. Reduce the numbers block before cutting the speaking activity.

---

## Core 60-Minute Plan

### Slide 1 — Welcome (5 min)
**Purpose:** Orient students; set the tone.
**Teacher notes:** Introduce yourself. Explain the format: live, small group, speaking starts today. Say: "Session 1 will feel like a lot of new sounds at once. That's normal. You don't need to master anything today — you just need to start."
**Student activity:** None — instructor speaking.

---

### Slide 2 — How French Sounds: Awareness Only (7 min)
**Purpose:** Give students a mental map of what feels different, without drilling everything.
**Teacher notes:**
- Name three things, demonstrate each once, move on. Students are listening, not repeating yet.
- **The r:** comes from the back of the throat, not the tongue tip. Say *Paris, merci, rouge*. Let students try once — just once. Say: "You'll keep working on this. It takes time."
- **Silent endings:** Most final consonants are silent. *Petit, vous, grand.* Demonstrate the silence.
- **Nasal vowels:** When you see *an, in, on* before a consonant, air goes through the nose. Say *bon, vin, blanc.* Let students hear it.
- Do not drill any of these. Awareness only. Full pronunciation practice comes in homework and future sessions.
**Student activity:** Listen and try to repeat once. No individual correction yet — just exposure.

---

### Slide 3 — Greetings (10 min)
**Purpose:** Teach the essential greetings and leave-takings; students can use them by the end of this slide.
**Teacher notes:**
- **Core greetings:** *Bonjour* (daytime, any context), *Bonsoir* (evening), *Salut* (informal).
- **Core leave-takings:** *Au revoir, À bientôt, Salut* (informal goodbye), *Bonne journée*.
- **Social exchanges:** *Comment tu vas? / Ça va?* → *Très bien, merci. Et toi? / Pas mal.*
- Teach *Enchanté/e* (nice to meet you). Flag the -e ending for women — do not explain adjective agreement yet; just model it.
- Choral repetition after each phrase. Then pairs: students greet each other. Listen for pronunciation and correct gently.
**Student activity:** Choral repetition → students greet each other in pairs (1 round formal, 1 round informal).

---

### Slide 4 — Tu vs. Vous (5 min)
**Purpose:** Students know which form to use before they start speaking.
**Teacher notes:**
- *Tu* = first-name basis. *Vous* = title basis, or addressing a group.
- In this class: *tu* with each other. Either is fine with the instructor.
- Common trap: students default to *vous* because it feels polite. Reassure them that *tu* with peers is correct, not rude.
- Quick scenarios: (a) your classmate → *tu*; (b) a bank teller → *vous*; (c) the whole group at once → *vous*.
**Student activity:** 3 scenarios — students call out *tu* or *vous*.

---

### Slide 5 — Je M'Appelle and Je Suis (12 min)
**Purpose:** Students leave with the two most essential phrases for introducing themselves.
**Teacher notes:**
- **Je m'appelle [name].** Teach as a fixed phrase. Do not explain reflexive verbs. If asked: "We'll cover why it works this way in Week 3 — for now just know the phrase."
- **Je suis de [city/country].** Introduce *je suis* as the first form of être students will use. Do not present the full conjugation table yet — that risks overwhelming Session 1. Just this one form for now.
- **J'habite à [city].** Introduce as a second phrase. Students often confuse *je suis de* (origin) and *j'habite à* (current residence) — demonstrate with a quick example: "Je suis de Lyon, mais j'habite à Paris."
- Model your complete mini-introduction: *Bonjour, je m'appelle Thomas. Je suis de New York. J'habite à New York.*
- Then: students repeat with their own information. Pairs first, then one volunteer to the group.
**Student activity:** Instructor models → students say their own version to a partner → one volunteer shares with the group.

---

### Slide 6 — First Speaking Activity (12 min)
**Purpose:** Students have a real conversation in French, however brief.
**Teacher notes:**
- Give each pair 4–5 minutes to practice the following exchange. Display it on screen.

> *— Bonjour ! Je m'appelle [name]. Et toi ?*
> *— Bonjour ! Je m'appelle [name]. Tu es d'où ?*
> *— Je suis de [city/country]. Et toi ?*
> *— Je suis de [city/country]. Enchanté/e !*
> *— Enchantée / Enchanté !*

- Do not require perfect pronunciation or grammar. Listen, note 1–2 errors per pair, address the most common errors with the whole group after.
- After pairs, ask 1 pair to demonstrate. Close with: "That was French. You just did that."
**Student activity:** Pair practice (4–5 min) → one pair presents → brief group debrief.

---

### Slide 7 — Numbers 1–20 (7 min)
**Purpose:** Build the number foundation students will use throughout the course.
**Teacher notes:**
- Count 1–20 together. Say each number, students repeat.
- Pronunciation notes to give as you go (briefly): *un* (nasal); *deux* (final x silent); *quatre* (the r); *sept* (the p is silent).
- Count together × 2. Then spot-drill: call out random numbers 1–20, students respond.
- Do not teach 21–30 live unless you finish Slide 6 early. It is in the homework.
**Student activity:** Choral count × 2 → random number drill.

---

### Slide 8 — Wrap-Up and Homework Preview (2 min)
**Purpose:** Close positively; set clear homework expectations.
**Teacher notes:** Say: "You spoke French today. That's what we're here to do. The homework is short — say your introduction out loud and do the numbers." Keep it upbeat.
**Student activity:** Students note homework. Optional: everyone says one French word before logging off.

---

## Cut First If Running Behind

If you are behind at any point, reduce or cut in this order:

1. **Slide 6 (speaking activity):** Shorten pair practice from 5 min to 3 min. Skip the presentation pair — just debrief.
2. **Slide 7 (numbers):** Reduce to numbers 1–10 live; assign 11–20 as homework alongside 21–30.
3. **Slide 4 (tu/vous scenarios):** Cut the scenarios; just state the rule and move on.
4. Do not cut Slides 5 or 6 entirely — students must speak before they leave.

---

## Extension for Fast Cohorts

Only if you finish the core plan with time remaining:

- **Alphabet:** Teach French letter names. Most useful first: *e, u, r, g, j, h*. Ask students to spell their first name. (~6 min)
- **Numbers 21–30:** Teach the pattern live (*vingt et un, vingt-deux...*) and count together. (~3 min)
- **Full introduction with nationality:** Add *Je suis [nationality]* to the introduction and drill nationality gender briefly. (~4 min)

---

## Move to Homework If Needed

These items do not require live class time in Session 1:

- Full alphabet with letter names (reference sheet in homework)
- Numbers 21–30 and the pattern for 31–100
- Nasal vowels practice (awareness was enough live; drill via repetition at home)
- The French r (exposure was enough; home practice with recorded examples)
- H aspiré vs. h muet (not needed for any Week 1 speaking task)
- Liaison as a system (point it out live when it occurs; do not explain the rules)
