# French A1 — Week 1 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 1 and 2
**Theme:** Sounds, Greetings, and First Introductions
**Week objective:** Students can greet someone in French, introduce themselves in 4–5 sentences, and hold a short two-way exchange. They leave Session 2 having spoken French out loud to the group.

---

## What Must Be Protected in Live Class Time

These items cannot be outsourced to homework — they require your voice, real-time modeling, and spoken response from students.

- **Être — spoken production.** Students need to say *je suis, tu es, il/elle est* out loud with correction before they leave Session 2. A conjugation table on paper is not enough.
- **The complete introduction.** By the end of Session 2, every student must give their full French introduction out loud to the group. This is non-negotiable. Build time for it.
- **Tu vs. vous — concrete scenarios.** Tell them when to use each. Students will use *vous* by default out of anxiety; they need permission to use *tu*.
- **The French r and nasal vowels.** Awareness in Session 1 only — students need to hear these sounds and try once before they start speaking. Do not drill them. They do not need to master them this week.

## Pacing Guidance

### Session 1 (60 min)

| Block | Content | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Welcome and setup | Course format, what to expect, tone | 5 min |
| Pronunciation awareness | The r, silent endings, nasal preview — hear and try once | 7 min |
| Greetings | Formal and informal, *enchanté/e* | 10 min |
| Tu vs. vous | Three scenarios, student call-outs | 5 min |
| First phrases | *Je m'appelle, je suis de, j'habite à* (one form of être only) | 12 min |
| First speaking activity | Pairs practice short exchange | 12 min |
| Numbers 1–20 | Choral count + spot drill | 7 min |
| Wrap-up and homework preview | | 2 min |

**Keep full être conjugation and the alphabet for homework.** Neither is needed to speak in Session 1, and either can consume 10+ minutes if you let it.

### Session 2 (60 min)

| Block | Content | Time |
|---|---|---|
| Warm-up | Review Session 1 phrases, hear pronunciation from home practice | 8 min |
| Être: 3 forms in context | *je suis / tu es / il-elle est* — teach from context, not table | 10 min |
| Nationality and profession | Students' own words only; no pre-loaded list | 8 min |
| Three question frames | *Tu t'appelles comment? / Tu es d'où? / Tu fais quoi dans la vie?* | 8 min |
| Dialogue practice | Read model, pair practice, brief debrief | 18 min |
| Full introduction round | Every student speaks — non-negotiable | 8 min |

**Numbers 30–100 are in the Session 2 homework, not in live class.** If you finish the introduction round with time left, you can offer numbers 30–69 (the regular pattern) as an extension — but do not let it eat into the introduction round.

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 1

| Mistake | Notes |
|---|---|
| *Je suis appelle X* (mixing *je suis* with *je m'appelle*) | Teach *je m'appelle* as a fixed phrase. Say: "We'll explain why it works this way in Week 3." |
| Pronouncing the final -s of *vous êtes* | Liaison: the -s sounds only before a vowel. *Vous êtes* — the s is voiced. Model it. |
| Using *vous* with everyone out of politeness | Explain that *tu* is normal and expected with peers. They need explicit permission to use it. |
| Pronouncing French *e* like English "ay" | Model the open è and closed é difference. Students will conflate them early. |
| Forgetting to feminize nationality/profession | *Elle est française,* not *Elle est français.* Flag it in Session 2 without over-explaining — adjective agreement is Week 2. |

## Suggested Instructor Language

- **For pronunciation awareness:** *"I'm going to say a few things that sound strange. You're just listening and trying once. Don't worry about getting it right — that's what the next twelve weeks are for."*
- **For être:** *"We're starting with three forms today. That's all you need for this week. The other three forms are in your homework — they're not hard, just more."*
- **For je m'appelle:** *"Don't ask me why it works this way yet. Just know the phrase — je m'appelle [name]. We'll explain the grammar in Week 3."*
- **For tu vs. vous:** *"Think of tu as the word you use with people you'd call by their first name. In this class, use tu with each other."*
- **For the introduction round:** *"You now know enough to introduce yourself in French. I want each of you to do it. I'll go first."*

---

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- The full alphabet in Session 1 (homework — students do not need it to speak in Week 1)
- H aspiré vs. h muet (mention it exists; give a reference; move on)
- Liaison rules as a system (demonstrate *vous êtes* — do not teach the rules)
- Question formation with inversion or *est-ce que* as a system — the three spoken frames from Session 2 are enough for all A1 conversation tasks
- Extended nationality and profession vocabulary — give them the list; drill only what students personally need

---

## What to Assign as Homework If Pacing Runs Long

If Session 1 runs out of time before numbers:
→ Numbers 1–20 are in the Session 1 homework. Assign them explicitly.

If Session 2 runs out of time before the introduction round:
→ Ask each student to record a 30-second voice introduction and send it before Session 3. This is not ideal — the group moment matters — but it is better than skipping it entirely.

Both homework files are pre-written and ready to distribute.

---

## Week 1 Priority Reminder

The single most important thing that happens in Week 1 is that every student **says something in French out loud** and feels okay about it. Accuracy is secondary to confidence at this stage. Correct gently, keep the energy high, and end both sessions with a moment where students feel they succeeded at something real.
