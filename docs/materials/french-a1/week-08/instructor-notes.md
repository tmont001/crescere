# French A1 — Week 8 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 15 and 16
**Theme:** Home, Neighborhood, and High-Frequency Irregular Verbs

---

## Week Objective

By the end of Week 8, students can:
- Use *il y a / il n'y a pas de* to describe what is (or isn't) in their home
- Name 8+ rooms and 8+ furniture/household items with correct gender
- Use prepositions of place (*sur, sous, devant, derrière, entre, à côté de, en face de*) to describe where things are, including *de + article* contractions (*du, de la, de l', des*)
- Describe their home in 5–6 spoken sentences
- Conjugate and use *faire, venir, prendre, savoir,* and *connaître* in context
- Distinguish *savoir* from *connaître*
- Describe their neighborhood using adjectives and location phrases

**Relationship to the curriculum:** This is exactly the Week 8 content specified. Session 15 = home vocabulary + *il y a* + prepositions. Session 16 = high-frequency irregular verbs + neighborhood. No deferral from Week 7 is carried into this week.

**Note on prepositions:** Some prepositions (*près de, loin de, à côté de, en face de*) were introduced in Week 5 (directions). Students have seen them but may not have them fully automatic. Session 15 reinforces these and adds the interior-space prepositions (*sur, sous, devant, derrière*) in a new context.

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 8

| Mistake | Correction | When to address |
|---|---|---|
| *Il n'y a pas d'un garage* (keeping article after negation) | *Il n'y a pas de garage.* Same rule as always: negation → *de*. | Session 15 *il y a* block |
| *La cuisine est il y a...* (confusing *il y a* with location verb) | *Il y a une cuisine.* (existence) vs. *La cuisine est en face du salon.* (location — use *être*). | Session 15 throughout |
| *La cuisine est en face de le salon* (not contracting) | *En face **du** salon.* *De + le = du* — mandatory contraction. | Session 15 prepositions block |
| *Il y a une cuisine devant le salon* vs. *La cuisine est devant le salon* | Both are grammatically possible; distinguish: *il y a* = announces existence; *être* = locates. But at A1 level, accept both and focus on flow. | If it arises |
| *Je fait* (wrong form) | *Je fais.* The stem is *fais* for je/tu; *fait* for il/elle. | Session 16 faire block |
| *Ils vienvent* (wrong form) | *Ils viennent.* The -nt ending attaches to *vienn-*, not *vien-*. | Session 16 venir block |
| *Je sais Paris* (savoir with a city) | *Je **connais** Paris.* Cities/places → *connaître*. *Savoir* is for facts and skills, not familiarity. | Session 16 savoir/connaître block |
| *Je connais que le train est en retard* (connaître with a clause) | *Je **sais** que le train est en retard.* *Connaître* cannot take a *que* clause — only *savoir* can. | Session 16 if it arises |

## Suggested Instructor Language

- **For *il y a*:** *"Il y a means 'there is' or 'there are' — and it never changes form. One bedroom: 'il y a une chambre.' Ten bedrooms: 'il y a dix chambres.' Same phrase. In the negative: 'il n'y a pas de garage' — same rule as all negation: after 'pas,' just use 'de.'"*

- **For *de + article* contractions:** *"When prepositions like 'à côté de' or 'en face de' are followed by 'le,' they contract: 'de + le = du.' So: 'à côté du salon,' not 'à côté de le salon.' This is mandatory in French — you can never say 'de le.' You've seen 'du' before in partitive articles (du café) and negation — it's the same du."*

- **For *faire*:** *"Faire is one of the most versatile verbs in French — it means 'to do,' 'to make,' and appears in weather expressions and activity phrases. The irregular forms to watch: 'vous faites' (not 'faisez') and 'ils font.' The rest — 'je fais, tu fais, il fait, nous faisons' — follow a predictable pattern once you hear them."*

- **For *savoir* vs. *connaître*:** *"The key test: if you can say 'I know that...' or 'I know how to...,' use savoir. If you can say 'I know [person] personally' or 'I'm familiar with [place],' use connaître. One memory trick: connaître contains 'connaiss-' which comes from the same root as 'reconnaissance' — recognizing something you've encountered before."*

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- *Venir de + infinitif* (to have just done something) — introduce *venir de Paris* only; the *venir de + inf* construction is extra
- More than 8–10 rooms and 8–10 furniture items — quality over quantity
- Extended neighborhood adjectives beyond 4–5 core ones (*calme, animé, bruyant, pratique, agréable*)
- *Prendre* extended uses (prendre une décision, prendre froid) — just the transport context
- Full *de + article* table — students know *du* from Week 6; introduce *du/de la/de l'/des* in preposition context without presenting it as a new system

## Cultural Context for Instructors

- **French apartment vocabulary:** French people commonly describe homes by *pièces* (rooms) — not counting the kitchen or bathroom. *Un deux-pièces* = kitchen + 1 bedroom + living room. *Un studio* = one room. Students visiting France should know that *un appartement avec deux pièces* is not two bedrooms — it's two main rooms (typically 1 bedroom + salon).
- **Rez-de-chaussée:** In France (and most of Europe), the ground floor is *le rez-de-chaussée* (floor 0), and the first floor above that is *le premier étage.* This is different from US/UK usage where "first floor" = ground floor for Americans.
- **Le quartier:** French people have a strong attachment to their *quartier* (neighborhood). Describing where in a city you live often means naming your *quartier* or nearby landmarks, not just the street address.
