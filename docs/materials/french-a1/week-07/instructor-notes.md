# French A1 — Week 7 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 13 and 14
**Theme:** Shopping, Clothing, and the Market

---

## Week Objective

By the end of Week 7, students can:
- Use demonstrative adjectives (*ce, cet, cette, ces*) correctly with clothing vocabulary
- Apply color adjective agreement (gender and number) with clothing items
- Use large numbers for prices; ask *C'est combien?* and understand simple responses
- Navigate a clothing shop interaction: finding an item, asking for a size, trying it on, buying or declining
- Use quantity expressions (*un kilo de, une bouteille de, une douzaine de...*) at a French market
- Complete a full market role play: asking for items, specifying quantities, asking prices, buying

**Relationship to the curriculum:** This is exactly the Week 7 content specified in the curriculum. Session 13 = clothing, demonstratives, colors, shopping dialogue. Session 14 = market scenario, quantity expressions, prices. No content is deferred from Week 6 into this week — Week 6 is closed.

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 7

| Mistake | Correction | When to address |
|---|---|---|
| *Ce imperméable* (wrong form before vowel) | *Cet imperméable.* The form *cet* is mandatory before a vowel or h muet. Correct every instance. | Session 13 demonstratives block |
| *Cette pantalon* (wrong gender) | *Ce pantalon.* Pantalon is masculine — *ce,* not *cette.* Students must know the gender of clothing nouns. | Session 13 demonstratives block |
| *Un robe rouge* (wrong article + missed agreement) | *Une robe rouge.* Robe is feminine — *une,* and the color takes no agreement change here since *rouge* is invariable. But *une robe verte* (not *vert*). | Session 13 colors block |
| *Des chaussures marrons* (adding -s to invariable color) | *Des chaussures marron.* Colors like *marron, orange, rose, beige* are invariable — they never take -s or feminine endings. | Session 13 colors block |
| *Un kilo du fromage* (keeping definite article after quantity) | *Un kilo **de** fromage.* After quantity expressions, the article disappears — just *de*. This is the same rule as after negation (*pas de*). | Session 14 quantities block |
| *Ça coûte combien euros* (wrong word order) | *Ça coûte combien?* or *C'est combien?* — the price question does not include *euros* inside the question form. | Session 14 prices block |
| Forgetting to change *des* to *de* after negation | *Je n'ai pas **de** croissants.* — same negation rule from Week 6. Reinforce across the week. | Any time |
| Clothing gender confusion | *une veste* (f.), *un manteau* (m.), *une robe* (f.), *un pull* (m.) — the gender must be learned with the word. Test as you go. | Session 13 vocabulary block |

## Suggested Instructor Language

- **For *cet* before vowels:** *"We use 'cet' instead of 'ce' when the masculine noun starts with a vowel or a silent h. It's there for pronunciation — 'ce imperméable' is hard to say; 'cet imperméable' flows better. Think of it like the article 'un' vs. 'l'' before vowels."*

- **For color invariable forms:** *"Some colors in French never change — they're borrowed words or compound colors: marron, orange, rose, beige. You can remember them because they all sound like nouns — maroon, orange, rose. When a color sounds like a noun, it's probably invariable."*

- **For quantity + de:** *"After any quantity word — un kilo, une bouteille, une douzaine — the article disappears and you just use 'de.' It's the same logic as after negation: 'pas de.' Whenever you're specifying an amount, French uses just 'de.' So: 'un kilo de fromage,' not 'un kilo du fromage.'"*

- **For the clothing role play:** *"When you walk into a shop in France, the shopkeeper will usually say 'Bonjour! Je peux vous aider?' — 'Can I help you?' Your answer is: 'Oui, je cherche... ' or 'Non merci, je regarde' ('I'm just looking'). In France, you say bonjour before anything else. Entering a shop without saying bonjour is considered rude."*

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- Numbers above 100 in detail — students can ask *C'est combien?* and understand simple number responses without knowing exact large-number formation
- *Les soldes* cultural detail — one sentence is enough; do not spend more than 1 minute on it
- More than 12 clothing items — students need to say them aloud and use them in context; a shorter list used actively beats a longer list learned passively
- *Cet* before h muet vs. h aspiré distinction — too fine a point for this week; just teach *cet* before all h-words and flag that the distinction exists

## Cultural Context for Instructors

- **Les soldes:** France has two official sale periods per year — January (*les soldes d'hiver*) and July (*les soldes d'été*). Outside these periods, discounting is regulated. The word *soldes* (plural, feminine) is always used in the plural: *les soldes commencent le 10 janvier.*
- **Le marché:** Open-air markets are a central part of French life. Most French towns have markets 1–3 days a week. The market typically offers local produce, cheese, meat, and clothing at competitive prices. Students visiting France will encounter them immediately.
- **La boulangerie vs. le supermarché:** Students may already know *la boulangerie* from Week 6. In Week 7 they should understand that the French often buy bread at a boulangerie, produce at a market, and packaged goods at a supermarché — rather than doing all shopping in one place.
- **Bonjour protocol:** Saying *bonjour* upon entering any shop and *au revoir* upon leaving is a strong social norm in France. Failing to greet is considered rude. Remind students of this when setting up role plays.
