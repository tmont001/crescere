# French A1 — Week 11 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 21 and 22
**Theme:** Health and the Body

---

## Week Objective

By the end of Week 11, students can:
- Name at least 12 body parts with correct gender and article
- Use *avoir mal à* correctly with *au/aux/à la/à l'* contractions
- Describe symptoms in a pharmacy context (*J'ai de la fièvre, je suis enrhumé(e), je tousse*)
- Ask a pharmacist for a product (*Vous avez quelque chose pour...? Je voudrais quelque chose pour...*)
- Use *il faut + infinitif* to give or understand general advice
- Use *devoir* to give or receive specific advice (*Vous devez vous reposer*)
- Express duration of symptoms using *depuis + présent*
- Handle a basic doctor's appointment role play end-to-end

**Relationship to the curriculum:** This is exactly the Week 11 content specified in the curriculum. Session 21 = body parts + *avoir mal à* + pharmacy scenario. Session 22 = *il faut + infinitif*, *devoir* for advice, *depuis + présent*, doctor's appointment role play.

**Prior knowledge students bring:**
- *Avoir* conjugation (Week 2 — avoir for age/possession)
- *Au/aux* contractions (Week 5 — aller + au/aux/à la)
- *Devoir* full conjugation (Week 9 — obligation)
- *Je voudrais* as a formula (Week 6 — restaurant ordering)
- *Il faut* mentioned in skip list of Week 9 — do not assume students have been drilled on it; teach fresh here

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 11

| Mistake | Correction | When to address |
|---|---|---|
| *J'ai mal à le dos* (forgetting contraction) | *J'ai mal **au** dos.* — *à + le = au*. Same contraction as *je vais au cinéma.* | Session 21, *avoir mal à* block |
| *J'ai mal les pieds* (dropping *à*) | *J'ai mal **aux** pieds.* — *à* is always there; it just contracts with *les.* | Session 21, *avoir mal à* block |
| *J'ai fièvre* (dropping article) | *J'ai **de la** fièvre.* — *fièvre* always takes partitive *de la*. This is a count noun in French, unlike English. | Session 21, symptoms block |
| *Je suis rhume* (treating *rhume* as state) | *Je suis **enrhumé(e)**.* — The adjective *enrhumé* goes with *être*, not the noun *rhume*. | Session 21, symptoms block |
| *Il faut vous reposer* (adding subject to *il faut*) | *Il faut se reposer.* — *Il faut* is always impersonal; it takes an infinitive, never a conjugated verb. | Session 22, *il faut* block |
| *Il faut que je repose* (subjunctive creep) | This is A2 grammar. At A1, *il faut + infinitif* only. *Il faut se reposer.* | Session 22 if it arises |
| *Depuis + passé composé* | *J'ai de la fièvre depuis trois jours.* NOT *J'ai eu de la fièvre depuis trois jours.* (which implies it's over). If the situation is still happening, stay in present. | Session 22, *depuis* block |
| *J'ai mal depuis trois jours à la gorge* (word order) | *J'ai mal à la gorge depuis trois jours.* — *depuis* phrase comes after the full pain expression, not between verb and body part. | Session 22 if it arises |

## Suggested Instructor Language

**For the *au/aux* contraction in *avoir mal à*:**
*"You already know this contraction — you've been using it since Week 5. 'Je vais au cinéma' — same 'au.' 'J'ai mal au dos' — same contraction. 'À + le' always becomes 'au.' The only new part is that 'avoir mal' takes the body part with its article, and the article contracts the same way."*

**For *avoir de la fièvre* vs. *avoir la fièvre*:**
*"In French, fever takes the partitive: 'j'ai de la fièvre.' Think of it like having some of a thing — you have some fever. English says 'I have a fever' with an indefinite article, but French uses partitive. This is the same partitive from Week 6 — 'j'ai du café, j'ai de la fièvre.' Same logic."*

**For *il faut* vs. *devoir*:**
*"'Il faut se reposer' is advice for anyone — general. 'Vous devez vous reposer' is what a doctor says to you specifically, right now. Both mean 'you need to rest,' but 'il faut' is impersonal — it applies to everyone in this situation. 'Devoir' is personal — it's directed at one person."*

**For *depuis + présent*:**
*"In English you say 'I've had a fever for three days' — past tense, because it started in the past. In French, you say 'j'ai de la fièvre depuis trois jours' — present tense, because you still have it NOW. If you're still in the situation, French stays in the present. This is one of the biggest differences from English when talking about duration."*

## What to Skip If Time Is Tight

- Extended doctor vocabulary beyond the role play script (*faire une piqûre, radiographie, analyses de sang*) — not A1 level
- *Il faut que + subjonctif* — this is A2. Only teach *il faut + infinitif*
- *Pendant* vs. *depuis* distinction in depth — just teach *depuis* for ongoing situations; note *pendant* exists for completed duration if asked
- Symptom vocabulary beyond the core list (12 body parts, 8 symptoms is enough for the pharmacy/doctor scenarios)
- *Avoir du mal à + infinitif* (to have difficulty doing something) — mention only if a student produces it naturally

## Preparing Students for the Week 12 Final Performance Task

Week 12 Part A (spoken role play, 5–7 min) includes meeting someone, making a plan, giving directions, and describing a place — all Week 1–10 content. Health is NOT required for the final spoken task per the curriculum's final performance description.

However, Week 11 provides two rehearsal benefits for Week 12:
1. **Depuis + présent** — the duration structure appears naturally in self-description ("I've been studying French for three months" = *j'apprends le français depuis trois mois*). Instructors should note this connection explicitly.
2. **Role play stamina** — by Session 22, students who have navigated a full pharmacy and doctor encounter without preparation notes are demonstrably more confident entering Week 12 extended role plays.

Week 12 Part B (written message, 8–10 sentences) may draw on health vocabulary if students want to include it, but the required topics are: introduce yourself, describe your daily routine, suggest a plan for getting together.

## Cultural Context for Instructors

**French healthcare system:** France has one of the most comprehensive public healthcare systems in the world. Students may ask why the scenarios use *vous* — doctor-patient interactions are almost universally formal in France, even with a familiar doctor over time. The green cross pharmacy sign is ubiquitous and pharmacists have significant advisory scope — they can recommend OTC treatments for many conditions without a prescription.

**Pharmacies and minor illness:** In France, the pharmacy is typically the first stop for minor illness. Sentences like *"J'ai un peu de fièvre — vous avez quelque chose?"* are extremely common. The pharmacist role in the Session 21 scenario is realistic.

**Depuis in everyday speech:** The *depuis + présent* structure comes up constantly in natural French conversation, well beyond health contexts: *j'habite ici depuis dix ans, je travaille ici depuis deux mois.* If time allows, briefly note this broader use — it reassures students that they are learning a high-frequency pattern, not a niche health formula.
