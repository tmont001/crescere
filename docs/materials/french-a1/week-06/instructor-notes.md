# French A1 — Week 6 Instructor Notes

**Sessions:** 11 and 12
**Theme:** Food, Cafés, and Restaurants

---

## Week Objective

By the end of Week 6, students can:
- Order food and drink politely in a French café using *je voudrais* and *je prends*
- Use partitive articles correctly (*du, de la, de l', des*) with food and drink vocabulary
- Apply the negation rule: partitive → *de* after *ne...pas*
- Conjugate *vouloir* and recognize *pouvoir*
- Navigate a full French restaurant interaction: asking questions, ordering, asking about dishes, requesting the bill
- Complete a 3–4 minute café or restaurant role play with a partner

**Relationship to the curriculum:** This is exactly the Week 6 content specified in the curriculum. Partitives and *je voudrais* are the grammatical core of Session 11; *vouloir/pouvoir* and the full restaurant scenario are Session 12. No deferral from Week 5 is carried into this week — Week 5 content (aller, directions, transportation) is now closed.

**Partitives note:** The curriculum instructor notes flag this as a persistent error source: "students will use *pas du* or *pas des* if not corrected early." Address the negation rule explicitly in Session 11 and correct every instance immediately.

## Common Student Mistakes — Week 6

| Mistake | Correction | When to address |
|---|---|---|
| *Je ne prends pas du café* (keeping partitive after negation) | *Je ne prends **pas de** café.* Partitive collapses to *de* after negation. Correct every time. | Session 11 negation block |
| *J'aime du chocolat* (partitive after aimer) | *J'aime **le** chocolat.* Definite article after *aimer/adorer/détester/préférer* — these verbs express a general preference and take the definite article, not the partitive. | Session 11 if it arises |
| *Je voudrais un café* (unnecessary article) | *Je voudrais **un** café* — actually correct for countable items. *Je voudrais **du** café* is correct for uncountable coffee (i.e., "some coffee"). Both occur in cafés; explain the distinction briefly. | Session 11 |
| *Des escargots pas bons* (wrong negation word order) | *Les escargots ne sont **pas** bons.* Negation wraps the verb: *ne...pas*. The noun must be the subject with its article. | Session 11/12 if it arises |
| *Je veux* (instead of *je voudrais*) | *Je veux* is grammatically correct but sounds blunt in formal settings. *Je voudrais* is always the polite default. Model it consistently. | Session 11 ordering block |
| *L'addition, s'il vous plaît* → forgotten or said *l'addition, merci* | No correction needed — saying *merci* alone is fine. But make sure *s'il vous plaît* is also practiced. | Session 12 role play |
| Wrong *vouloir* forms (*je veut, ils veux*) | *je veux, ils veulent* — irregular verb; drill the full table. | Session 12 conjugation block |
| Countable/uncountable confusion | In French, some items are always countable (*un croissant, une baguette*) — use *un/une*, not partitive. Other items are uncountable as typically ordered (*du café, de la bière*). | Session 11 vocabulary block |

## Suggested Instructor Language

- **For introducing partitives:** *"In French, you cannot just say 'I want coffee' — you need to say 'I want some coffee.' French makes this explicit with partitive articles. 'Some coffee' is 'du café' because coffee is masculine. 'Some beer' is 'de la bière' because beer is feminine. 'Some water' is 'de l'eau' because eau starts with a vowel. The logic is simple — you just need to know the gender of the noun."*

- **For the negation rule:** *"Here's the good news: after negation, all four forms simplify to just 'de'. No need to think about gender or vowels. 'Je ne prends pas de café, je ne prends pas de bière, je ne prends pas d'eau' — always 'de'. Some students want to say 'pas du café' — that's wrong. After 'pas,' it's always 'de.'"*

- **For *je voudrais*:** *"'Je voudrais' is the polite way to order in France. Literally it means 'I would like' — it uses a form called the conditional, which you'll study fully in A2. For now, learn it as a fixed expression. It's the most important phrase this week. If students aren't sure what to say in a café, 'Je voudrais...' covers almost everything."*

- **For the role play:** *"In France, the waiter will come to you. You don't need to catch their eye or wave. The waiter asks 'Qu'est-ce que vous prenez?' or 'Qu'est-ce que je vous sers?' — either phrase means 'What are you having?' Your response is 'Je voudrais...' or 'Je vais prendre...' When you're done, you ask for the bill: 'L'addition, s'il vous plaît.' That's the full loop."*

- More than 5–6 food items at the café — quality over quantity
- Complex menu items (*la bouillabaisse, le coq au vin*) — save for cultural enrichment, not core vocabulary
- *Le service est compris* / cultural tipping notes — assign as reading or mention briefly
- *Prendre* full conjugation — introduce as a useful ordering verb; full study is Week 8

## Cultural Context for Instructors

- **Le menu vs. la carte:** *Le menu* in France is a fixed-price meal deal (entrée + plat, or plat + dessert, or all three). *La carte* is the full à la carte menu. Many French restaurants offer both. Students ordering *"le menu"* are ordering the set meal, not just asking for the menu.
- **L'entrée = starter:** The false cognate: *une entrée* in French is the first course (starter), not the main course. The main course is *le plat principal*. This surprises many English speakers.
- **Service included:** In France, a 15% service charge is typically included in restaurant prices (*service compris* or *s.c.* on the bill). Additional tipping is optional and typically 5–10% for good service.
- **Café culture:** French cafés are social spaces. Standing at the bar (*au comptoir*) is cheaper than sitting at a table. Café orders are often paid at the end in brasseries, but at the time of ordering in some establishments. *Un café* always means espresso; if students want a filter coffee, they should ask for *un café filtre* or *un café américain*.
