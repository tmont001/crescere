# French B1 — Intermediate

**Language:** French
**Level:** B1 (CEFR Intermediate)
**Course ID:** fr-b1
**Status:** Approved draft — not yet taught

---

## Course Purpose

Develops genuine conversational independence. Students enter able to narrate the past and discuss future plans but cannot yet express nuance, hypothesis, or complex opinion. This course builds the subjunctive, conditional, and the grammatical infrastructure for abstract discussion — the structures that separate a functional speaker from a confident one. Students exit able to engage on professional, social, and cultural topics in a register appropriate to the situation.

---

## Ideal Student Profile

Completed French A2 or placed into B1 by placement test. Tells stories and holds basic conversations. Struggles with the subjunctive, conditional, and expressing nuance or hypothetical ideas. Motivated by professional use, travel at conversational depth, or cultural engagement.

---

## Prerequisites

French A2 or demonstrated A2-level competency.

---

## End-of-Course Outcomes

By the end of French B1, students can:

- [ ] Use the present subjunctive with at least 8 common triggers
- [ ] Use the conditional for polite requests and hypothetical situations
- [ ] Construct si-clause hypotheticals: si + imparfait → conditionnel (present hypothesis)
- [ ] Construct si-clause past regret: si + plus-que-parfait → conditionnel passé
- [ ] Use complex relative clauses: dont, où, lequel/laquelle and forms after prepositions
- [ ] Use reported speech with tense shifts
- [ ] Discuss abstract topics: politics, environment, social issues, career
- [ ] Switch between formal and informal registers appropriately
- [ ] Use B1-level idiomatic French in conversation
- [ ] Write a structured 200–250 word argumentative essay
- [ ] Understand French at near-natural speed on familiar topics

## 24-Session Breakdown

### Week 1 — A2 Consolidation and B1 Roadmap

**Session 1 — Gap Review and Plus-Que-Parfait**
- Focus: Targeted gap assessment through conversation; identify where each student's A2 grammar is unstable
- Grammar: Plus-que-parfait (had done): avoir/être (imparfait) + past participle; uses: past before past in a narrative; J'avais déjà mangé quand ils sont arrivés
- Speaking: Complex stories using three tenses: plus-que-parfait + imparfait + passé composé
- Session-end goal: Student can form plus-que-parfait and use it to sequence past events

**Session 2 — B1 Grammar Map**
- Focus: Orientation to what B1 unlocks — the subjunctive concept introduced
- Grammar: Subjunctive preview: the indicative states facts (je sais que tu viens); the subjunctive signals attitude (je veux que tu viennes); the rule: subjunctive required when two subjects + trigger verb/conjunction
- Speaking: Identifying contexts where English uses subjunctive invisibly ("I wish he were here", "It's important that she be present"); French makes the distinction explicit with a distinct form
- Session-end goal: Student understands the conceptual distinction between indicative and subjunctive

---

### Week 2 — The Subjunctive (Part 1)

**Session 3 — Present Subjunctive: Formation and Main Triggers**
- Grammar: Formation: take 3rd-person plural present → drop -ent → add -e/-es/-e/-ions/-iez/-ent; regular formation covers most verbs; irregular subjunctives (must be memorized): être → soit, avoir → ait, aller → aille, faire → fasse, pouvoir → puisse, vouloir → veuille, savoir → sache, venir → vienne; main wish/desire/need triggers: vouloir que, désirer que, souhaiter que, espérer que (note: espérer que usually takes indicative in practice — flag this)
- Speaking: Expressing wishes for yourself and others: Je veux que tu finisses, Il faut que vous soyez là
- Session-end goal: Student can form subjunctive for regular and top-10 irregular verbs; uses wish/desire triggers

**Session 4 — Subjunctive with Emotion and Impersonal Expressions**
- Grammar: Emotional reaction triggers: être content(e) que, être triste que, regretter que, avoir peur que; impersonal expressions: il faut que, il est important que, il est possible que, il est dommage que, il vaut mieux que, c'est bien que; dommage que + subjonctif as a high-frequency natural expression
- Speaking: Real-life expressions of concern, importance, and reaction; student-generated sentences
- Session-end goal: Student can use subjunctive with emotion triggers and impersonal expressions

---

### Week 3 — The Subjunctive (Part 2)

**Session 5 — Subjunctive After Conjunctions of Purpose, Concession, and Condition**
- Grammar: Purpose: pour que, afin que; concession/condition: bien que, quoique (both + subjunctive), à moins que, à condition que, sans que; comparison: bien que vs. même si (même si takes indicative); this distinction is tested in placement test
- Speaking: Expressing nuance: Je le ferai bien que je ne veuille pas, Nous partirons à moins qu'il ne pleuve
- Session-end goal: Student can use the six most common subjunctive conjunctions accurately

**Session 6 — Subjunctive with Temporal Conjunctions; Subj vs. Infinitif**
- Grammar: Temporal: avant que + subjunctive; après que (technically indicative in careful usage, but widespread subjunctive in spoken French — acknowledge both); jusqu'à ce que + subj; key distinction: subjunctive vs. infinitive — same subject takes infinitive (Je veux partir), different subjects require que + subjunctive (Je veux que tu partes)
- Speaking: Making plans with temporal markers; same-subject vs. different-subject drill pairs
- Session-end goal: Student reliably distinguishes subj/infinitif by subject; uses avant que and jusqu'à ce que

---

### Week 4 — The Conditional

**Session 7 — Conditionnel Présent: Formation and Polite Register**
- Grammar: Conditionnel = futur stem + imparfait endings (-ais/-ais/-ait/-ions/-iez/-aient); irregular stems same as futur: être → ser-, avoir → aur-, aller → ir-, faire → fer-, pouvoir → pourr-, vouloir → voudr-, venir → viendr-; polite use: Je voudrais, Je pourrais, Pourriez-vous...; indirect advice: à ta place je..., tu devrais..., il faudrait que...
- Speaking: Professional courtesy practice; advice-giving; Je voudrais un renseignement, Pourriez-vous m'aider?
- Session-end goal: Student uses conditionnel for polite requests and advice naturally

**Session 8 — Si + Imparfait → Conditionnel (Present Hypothesis)**
- Grammar: Si-clause for unreal present: Si + imparfait → conditionnel présent; imparfait in si-clause is NOT past tense here — it signals unreality; Si j'avais plus de temps, j'étudierais plus; common error: *Si j'aurais... — this is never correct in standard French
- Speaking: Hypothetical conversations: Si tu pouvais vivre n'importe où..., Si vous gagniez à la loterie...; extended pair discussions
- Session-end goal: Student constructs present-hypothesis si-clauses without the common avoir-in-si-clause error

---

### Week 5 — Advanced Hypotheticals and Reported Speech

**Session 9 — Conditionnel Passé and Past Regret Si-Clauses**
- Grammar: Conditionnel passé = conditionnel of avoir/être + past participle; Si + plus-que-parfait → conditionnel passé (past regret/counterfactual): Si j'avais su, j'aurais agi autrement; expressions of regret: j'aurais dû, j'aurais pu, il aurait fallu
- Speaking: Expressing regret and counterfactuals; discussion of decisions that could have gone differently
- Session-end goal: Student can construct past-regret si-clauses and express regret with conditionnel passé

**Session 10 — Reported Speech**
- Grammar: Reported speech tense shifts: dit que + (present → imparfait, PC → plus-que-parfait, futur → conditionnel); demandé si + (yes/no questions); demandé de + infinitif (command reporting); pronouns and time expressions shift (hier → la veille, demain → le lendemain)
- Speaking: Retelling conversations; news story reported speech; relaying messages
- Session-end goal: Student can report what someone said with appropriate tense shift

---

### Week 6 — Complex Relative Clauses

**Session 11 — Dont and Où**
- Grammar: Dont: replaces de + noun (after verbs and expressions that take de: avoir besoin de, parler de, se souvenir de, être content de, etc.); C'est le livre dont j'avais besoin; Où: replaces dans/à/en + place: La ville où j'habitais; time expressions: le jour où, l'année où
- Speaking: Describing things and places using dont and où in connected speech
- Session-end goal: Student can use dont correctly with verbs that take de, and où for place/time

**Session 12 — Lequel/Laquelle and Forms After Prepositions**
- Grammar: Lequel/laquelle/lesquels/lesquelles after prepositions (other than de, which uses dont): Le stylo avec lequel j'écris; La raison pour laquelle je suis venu; Contraction: lequel → auquel, lesquels → auxquels, lesquelles → auxquelles (à + le/les forms); De + lequel → duquel (for cases that need duquel, not dont)
- Speaking: Complex descriptions and explanations using all relative pronoun types
- Session-end goal: Student can use the full lequel/laquelle paradigm after prepositions

---

### Week 7 — Argumentation and Register

**Session 13 — Structured Argumentation in French**
- Grammar: Argumentation connectors: D'une part... d'autre part, en revanche, cependant, néanmoins, malgré tout, par conséquent, c'est pourquoi, ainsi, en outre, qui plus est, en ce qui concerne, quant à, force est de constater que
- Speaking: Structured debate practice: thesis → development → counterargument acknowledgment → conclusion; French debate style (more direct than English academic writing)
- Session-end goal: Student can structure a 3-minute argument in French using connectors

**Session 14 — Formal Register and Professional Email**
- Grammar: Formal register markers: Je me permets de vous écrire, Suite à notre entretien, Veuillez agréer, Dans l'attente de votre réponse, Je vous prie d'agréer l'expression de mes sentiments distingués; vous vs. tu extended discussion
- Speaking: Code-switching practice: same content, informal register → formal register; writing a formal email
- Session-end goal: Student can write and speak in an appropriate formal French register

---

### Week 8 — Professional and Business French

**Session 15 — Business Vocabulary and Meeting Scenarios**
- Grammar: Passive voice introduced as a tool in formal/business writing: Le rapport a été préparé, La décision a été prise; formal impersonal on
- Speaking: Business meeting simulation; expressing agreement/disagreement professionally; negotiation phrases
- Vocabulary: Business vocabulary (~40 words): une entreprise, un PDG, un bilan, un budget, un appel d'offres, une réunion d'équipe
- Session-end goal: Student can participate in a simulated business meeting in French

**Session 16 — Authentic Text and Professional Writing**
- Focus: Reading and discussing an authentic French business article or press release
- Speaking: Summarizing and reacting to the text; professional email writing practice
- Session-end goal: Student can read an authentic professional French text and discuss it

---

### Week 9 — Current Affairs and Social Topics

**Session 17 — Social and Political Vocabulary**
- Grammar: Reported speech reinforced with news-reporting structures: selon, d'après, il semblerait que (+ subj), les experts estiment que
- Speaking: Discussing social issues: la démocratie, l'environnement, l'immigration, l'égalité des chances, les droits de l'homme; expressing nuanced positions
- Vocabulary: Political and social vocabulary (~40 words); French and Francophone current issues
- Session-end goal: Student can discuss current events and social issues in French with appropriate vocabulary

**Session 18 — News Discussion and Authentic Audio**
- Focus: Authentic French news clip or podcast segment; discussion and analysis
- Speaking: Identifying main argument, evidence, and viewpoint; structured response; peer debate on the issue
- Session-end goal: Student can follow a near-authentic French audio source and discuss its content

---

### Week 10 — Idioms and Colloquial French

**Session 19 — High-Frequency Idioms**
- Grammar: Review of informal register; tu vs. vous in casual vs. formal contexts
- Speaking: Idiom introduction and practice: avoir le cafard (to feel down), s'en ficher (not to care), casser les pieds (to annoy), mettre les pieds dans le plat (to put your foot in it), avoir du pain sur la planche (to have a lot on your plate), faire la grasse matinée (to sleep in), tomber dans les pommes (to faint), c'est la croix et la bannière (it's a real ordeal), ça ne casse pas trois pattes à un canard (nothing special), avoir le cœur sur la main (to be generous); 10 idioms with drilling in natural context
- Vocabulary: ~30 additional high-frequency idioms introduced for recognition
- Session-end goal: Student can use 10 common idioms naturally and recognizes 30+ more

**Session 20 — Informal Register and Spoken French Patterns**
- Grammar: Spoken French reductions: je ne → j'(ne), tu es → t'es, il y a → y'a, qu'est-ce que → c'est quoi; register-switching between formal and informal
- Speaking: Casual conversation using idioms and informal patterns; contrast with the formal register from Week 7
- Session-end goal: Student can switch between formal and informal registers fluidly

---

### Week 11 — Written Argument

**Session 21 — Essay Structure**
- Grammar: Paragraph structure: introduction with thesis, body with argument and counterargument, conclusion; transition sentences between paragraphs; French essay conventions (plan dialectique: thèse / antithèse / synthèse)
- Writing focus: Structure a 2-paragraph response to an opinion prompt on a professional or cultural topic
- Session-end goal: Student produces a structurally correct 2-paragraph French argument

**Session 22 — Peer Editing and Self-Correction**
- Grammar: Common B1 writing errors identified and drilled: subjunctive avoidance, tense confusion, anglicisms, direct translation of English idioms
- Writing focus: Revision and polish of a written piece; peer feedback
- Session-end goal: Student can identify and correct their own B1-level errors in writing

---

### Week 12 — Review and Final Performance

**Session 23 — Integrated Oral Practice**
- Focus: Extended debate and discussion combining all B1 structures; spontaneous questions on cultural and professional topics; fluency-first approach with repair encouraged
- Speaking: Debate format; free conversation; timed speaking tasks

**Session 24 — Final Assessment and B2 Preview**
- Final performance tasks (see below)
- Preview of B2 structures: past subjunctive, passive voice deepened, literary register
- Independent study resources for B2 preparation

## Final Performance Task

**Part A — Structured debate (7–10 minutes):**
Student presents and defends a position on one of: technology and privacy, remote work, immigration, or a French/Francophone cultural issue. Must deploy subjunctive, conditional, and relative clauses naturally. Assessed on argumentation structure, grammar range, and register.

**Part B — Formal written essay (200–250 words):**
Two-paragraph argument with thesis, development (argument + counterargument acknowledgment), and conclusion. Graded on structure, grammar sophistication, vocabulary range, and register consistency.

## Readiness Criteria for B2

A student is ready for B2 when they can:

- [ ] Use present subjunctive accurately with 8+ common triggers
- [ ] Construct si-clause hypotheticals in both present and past forms
- [ ] Sustain a structured argument in French for 5+ minutes without preparation
- [ ] Read an authentic French text with ~80% comprehension
- [ ] Write a cohesive 200+ word structured essay
- [ ] Understand French at near-natural speed on familiar topics with minimal repetition

## Instructor Notes

- **The subjunctive (Weeks 2–3) is the course's most challenging block.** Students who miss Sessions 3–6 may never fully catch up. Consider making these sessions foundational: do not rush through them. The "same subject → infinitif / different subject → que + subj" rule from Session 6 is the single most practically useful distinction.
- **Si + imparfait (Week 4, Session 8):** The most common error is using conditionnel in the si-clause (*si j'aurais). Drill this explicitly and repeatedly. It appears on the placement test (fr-13) and is a B1 marker.
- **Espérer que (Session 3):** Standard grammar prescribes indicative after espérer que; spoken/informal French often uses subjunctive. Acknowledge both; recommend indicative for formal writing.
- **Après que (Session 6):** Technically takes indicative; spoken French widely uses subjunctive. Same approach: acknowledge both, recommend indicative.
- **Session 22 (peer editing) is genuinely useful.** Students who only receive instructor feedback develop a blind spot for self-correction. The peer editing session breaks that pattern.
- **B2 preview in Session 24:** Keep this brief — introduce the concept of past subjunctive and literary tenses but do not attempt teaching. The goal is to give motivated students a sense of what comes next.
