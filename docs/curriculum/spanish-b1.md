# Spanish B1 — Intermediate

**Language:** Spanish
**Level:** B1 (CEFR Intermediate)
**Course ID:** es-b1
**Status:** Approved draft — not yet taught

---

## Course Purpose

Develops genuine conversational independence in Spanish. Students enter able to narrate the past and discuss future plans but cannot yet express nuance, hypothesis, or complex opinion. This course builds the subjunctive, conditional, passive voice, and the vocabulary infrastructure for abstract discussion. Spanish B1 differs from French B1 in several important ways: the imperfecto de subjuntivo formation is non-obvious and requires dedicated instruction; the se pasivo and se impersonal constructions have no direct French equivalent; and the regional variation thread (begun in A2) continues with political, social, and professional vocabulary. Students exit able to engage on professional, social, and cultural topics across Spanish-speaking contexts.

---

## Ideal Student Profile

Completed Spanish A2 or placed into B1 by placement test. Confident with all past tenses and the future. Struggles with the subjunctive, conditional, and complex sentence structures. Motivated by professional use, immersion, or deeper engagement with Spanish-speaking cultures.

---

## Prerequisites

Spanish A2 or demonstrated A2-level competency.

---

## End-of-Course Outcomes

By the end of Spanish B1, students can:

- [ ] Use the present subjunctive with at least 8 common trigger types
- [ ] Use the imperfecto de subjuntivo in si-clause hypotheticals
- [ ] Use the conditional for polite requests, hypotheticals, and advice
- [ ] Construct si-clause hypotheticals: si + imperfecto de subjuntivo → conditional (present hypothesis)
- [ ] Construct si-clause past regret: si + pluscuamperfecto de subjuntivo → conditional perfecto
- [ ] Use complex relative clauses: que, quien, lo que, donde
- [ ] Use the passive voice: ser + past participle; se pasivo and se impersonal
- [ ] Discuss abstract topics: politics, environment, social issues, career
- [ ] Switch between formal and informal registers appropriately
- [ ] Use idiomatic Spanish naturally in conversation
- [ ] Write a structured argumentative response of 200–250 words
- [ ] Understand Spanish at near-natural speed on familiar topics

## 24-Session Breakdown

### Week 1 — A2 Consolidation and B1 Roadmap

**Session 1 — Gap Review and Pluscuamperfecto**
- Focus: Targeted gap assessment through conversation; locate unstable A2 grammar
- Grammar: Pluscuamperfecto (past perfect): había/habías/había/habíamos/habíais/habían + past participle; uses: past-before-past; Cuando llegué, ya habían comido; combining all past tenses in a complex narrative
- Speaking: Complex past stories using three tenses: pluscuamperfecto + imperfecto + pretérito
- Session-end goal: Student can form pluscuamperfecto and use it to sequence past events accurately

**Session 2 — Subjunctive Concept Introduction**
- Focus: Orientation to what B1 unlocks
- Grammar: Subjunctive preview — indicative states facts; subjunctive signals attitude (wish, doubt, emotion, judgment); the structural rule: requires que + different subject + trigger; comparing English (the subjunctive is largely invisible: "I want him to go" vs. Spanish quiero que vaya — the mood is explicit)
- Speaking: Identifying subjunctive triggers in spoken Spanish; hearing the form in authentic speech
- Session-end goal: Student understands the conceptual function of the subjunctive before encountering its form

---

### Week 2 — The Subjunctive (Part 1)

**Session 3 — Present Subjunctive: Formation and Main Triggers**
- Grammar: Formation rule: take present indicative yo form → drop -o → add opposite vowel endings (-ar verbs take -e endings: hable/hables/hable/hablemos/habléis/hablen; -er/-ir verbs take -a endings: coma/comas...). This is the key anchor: *the subjunctive uses the opposite vowel from the indicative.* Irregular subjunctives derived from irregular yo: tener → tenga, hacer → haga, venir → venga, decir → diga, poder → pueda, querer → quiera, saber → sepa; completely irregular: ser → sea/seas/sea/seamos/seáis/sean; estar → esté/estés/esté/estemos/estéis/estén; ir → vaya/vayas/vaya/vayamos/vayáis/vayan; haber → haya; main wish/need/desire triggers: querer que, desear que, esperar que, necesitar que
- Speaking: Expressing wishes and needs: Quiero que llegues a tiempo, Es necesario que estudies más
- Session-end goal: Student can form subjunctive for regular and top-10 irregular verbs; uses wish/need triggers

**Session 4 — Subjunctive with Emotion and Impersonal Expressions**
- Grammar: Emotional reaction triggers: alegrarse de que, sentir que, temer que, sorprender que, molestar que; impersonal expressions: es importante que, es necesario que, es posible que, es una lástima que, es mejor que, ojalá + subjunctive (no que needed); the ojalá form is high-frequency in both Spain and Latin America
- Speaking: Real expressions of concern, importance, and reaction; student-generated sentences on current situations
- Session-end goal: Student can use subjunctive with emotion triggers and impersonal expressions

---

### Week 3 — The Subjunctive (Part 2)

**Session 5 — Subjunctive with Temporal Conjunctions**
- Grammar: Temporal conjunctions: cuando + subjunctive when referring to future events (Cuando llegues, llámame; Cuando seas mayor, entenderás); hasta que + subj for future end-point; en cuanto + subj; antes de que + subj; critical note: **using indicative after cuando for future events is the single most common B1 error in Spanish** — *Cuando llegas (wrong) vs. Cuando llegues (correct) — drill this explicitly and repeatedly
- Speaking: Making plans and conditions; sequential future with cuando; everyday temporal expressions
- Session-end goal: Student reliably uses subjunctive after temporal conjunctions for future events

**Session 6 — Subjunctive with Purpose, Concession, and Subj vs. Infinitivo**
- Grammar: Purpose: para que, a fin de que; concession/condition: aunque (when uncertain/hypothetical — takes indicative when stating a fact), a menos que, con tal de que, sin que; key distinction: subjunctive vs. infinitive — same subject → infinitive (quiero ir), different subjects → que + subjunctive (quiero que vayas); emotional subjunctive: me alegra que, me molesta que, me preocupa que
- Speaking: Expressing nuance and conditions; paired same-subject/different-subject drills
- Session-end goal: Student uses subjunctive with purpose and concession conjunctions; applies subj/infinitivo rule

---

### Week 4 — The Conditional and Si-Clauses

**Session 7 — Conditional: Formation and Polite Register**
- Grammar: Conditional formation: infinitivo + -ía/-ías/-ía/-íamos/-íais/-ían; irregular stems (same as futuro): tener → tendr-, poder → podr-, hacer → har-, salir → saldr-, venir → vendr-, querer → querr-, decir → dir-, saber → sabr-, haber → habr-, poner → pondr-; polite use: ¿Podría/podría usted...? Me gustaría...; ¿Sería posible...?; advice: yo en tu lugar..., deberías..., lo ideal sería...
- Speaking: Professional courtesy and advice scenarios; polite requests; indirect advice
- Session-end goal: Student uses conditional for polite requests and advice naturally

**Session 8 — Imperfecto de Subjuntivo and Si-Clauses**
- Grammar: Imperfecto de subjuntivo formation — **take the preterite 3rd-person plural form, drop -ron, add -ra endings**: hablaron → habla- + ra/ras/ra/ramos/rais/ran; comieron → come- + ra...; fueran → fue- + ra...; tuvieran → tuvie- + ra...; the derivation from preterite 3rd plural means all irregularities carry over; si + imperfecto de subjuntivo → conditional (present/hypothetical): Si tuviera más tiempo, estudiaría más; Si viviera en México, hablaría español todo el día; common error: *Si tendría — conditional is never used in the si-clause
- Speaking: Hypothetical conversations: Si pudieras vivir en cualquier país..., Si ganaras la lotería...; extended pair discussions
- Session-end goal: Student can form imperfecto de subjuntivo and construct present-hypothesis si-clauses without the conditional-in-si-clause error

---

### Week 5 — Advanced Hypotheticals and Reported Speech

**Session 9 — Conditional Perfecto and Past Regret Si-Clauses**
- Grammar: Conditional perfecto = conditional of haber + past participle: habría/habrías/habría/habríamos/habríais/habrían + past participle; si + pluscuamperfecto de subjuntivo (hubiera + past participle) → conditional perfecto (past regret/counterfactual): Si hubiera sabido, lo habría hecho diferente; expressions of regret: habría debido, habría podido, ojalá hubiera...
- Speaking: Expressing regret and counterfactuals; discussing past decisions
- Session-end goal: Student can construct past-regret si-clauses and express regret with conditional perfecto

**Session 10 — Reported Speech**
- Grammar: Reported speech tense shifts: dijo que + (present → imperfecto, preterite → pluscuamperfecto, future → conditional); preguntó si + (yes/no questions with same tense shift); pidió que + imperfecto de subjuntivo (reporting a command); time expression shifts: hoy → ese día, ayer → el día anterior, mañana → al día siguiente
- Speaking: Retelling conversations; news-style reporting; relaying messages
- Session-end goal: Student can report what someone said with appropriate tense shift

---

### Week 6 — Complex Relative Clauses and Passive Voice

**Session 11 — Complex Relative Clauses**
- Grammar: que (subject and object); quien/quienes (after prepositions for people): la persona con quien trabajo; lo que (= the thing that — very high frequency: Lo que me sorprende es..., Todo lo que dice es verdad); donde (location): el barrio donde crecí; restrictive vs. non-restrictive clauses; que vs. quien with prepositions
- Speaking: Complex descriptions and explanations; opinions with lo que structures
- Session-end goal: Student can use que, quien, lo que, and donde in natural speech

**Session 12 — Passive Voice; Se Pasivo and Se Impersonal**
- Grammar: Passive with ser: El libro fue escrito por García Márquez (agent expressed with por); se pasivo (agentless passive for things): Se vende(n), Se habla español, Se prohíbe fumar; se impersonal (for actions without a specific agent, with people): Se dice que..., Se sabe que..., Se espera que (+ subjunctive); distinguishing se pasivo (verb agrees with grammatical subject) from se impersonal (always singular verb); these constructions have no direct French equivalent — address this explicitly
- Speaking: Describing processes, rules, and public notices using se; discussing what is said, known, and expected
- Session-end goal: Student can use se pasivo and se impersonal correctly and distinguish them

---

### Week 7 — Argumentation and Register

**Session 13 — Structured Argumentation in Spanish**
- Grammar: Argumentation connectors: por un lado... por otro lado, sin embargo, no obstante, en cambio, a pesar de eso, es cierto que, cabe destacar que, en definitiva, en cuanto a, hay que tener en cuenta que, dicho esto, en resumen; structured debate: planteamiento → desarrollo → concesión → refutación → conclusión
- Speaking: Structured debate practice; building and defending a position on a social issue
- Session-end goal: Student can structure a 3-minute argument in Spanish using connectors

**Session 14 — Formal Register and Professional Email**
- Grammar: Formal register markers: Estimada/o Sr./Sra..., Me dirijo a usted para..., Le escribo con motivo de..., Adjunto..., En espera de su respuesta, Le saluda atentamente; formal vs. colloquial register comparison; usted throughout professional contexts; regional formal register differences
- Speaking: Code-switching practice; writing a formal email
- Session-end goal: Student can write and speak in appropriate formal Spanish register

---

### Week 8 — Professional and Business Spanish

**Session 15 — Business Vocabulary and Meeting Scenarios**
- Grammar: Conditional and subjunctive in professional contexts; formal expressions of disagreement and compromise
- Speaking: Business meeting simulation; una negociación, una propuesta, un presupuesto, los recursos humanos, la cadena de suministro, el balance, un informe; professional agreement/disagreement
- Vocabulary: Business vocabulary (~40 words)
- Session-end goal: Student can participate in a simulated business meeting in Spanish

**Session 16 — Authentic Text and Professional Writing**
- Focus: Reading and discussing an authentic Spanish-language business article or press release
- Speaking: Summarizing and reacting to the text; professional email writing practice
- Session-end goal: Student can read an authentic professional Spanish text and discuss it

---

### Week 9 — Current Affairs and Social Topics

**Session 17 — Social and Political Vocabulary**
- Grammar: Reported speech with news-reporting structures: según, de acuerdo con, al parecer, se espera que (+ subj), el gobierno anunció que (+ imperfecto)
- Speaking: Social and political vocabulary: la democracia, el medio ambiente, la desigualdad, la migración, el desarrollo sostenible, los derechos humanos; issues framed across Spain and Latin America contexts; expressing nuanced position
- Vocabulary: Political and social vocabulary (~40 words); regional framing differences
- Session-end goal: Student can discuss current events and social issues with appropriate vocabulary

**Session 18 — News Discussion and Authentic Audio**
- Focus: Authentic Spanish news clip or podcast (one Spain-based, one Latin America-based); discussion and analysis
- Speaking: Identifying main argument, evidence, and viewpoint; structured group discussion and debate
- Session-end goal: Student can follow near-authentic Spanish audio and discuss its content

---

### Week 10 — Idioms and Colloquial Spanish

**Session 19 — High-Frequency Spanish Idioms**
- Grammar: Review of informal register; tú vs. usted in casual vs. formal contexts
- Speaking: Idiom introduction and drilling: no tener pelos en la lengua (to be blunt), echar de menos (to miss), estar hasta las narices (to be fed up), tener mala leche (to be in a bad mood), no hay mal que por bien no venga (every cloud has a silver lining), a grandes rasgos (broadly speaking), en el fondo (deep down), ponerse las pilas (to get your act together), ser un cero a la izquierda (to be worthless/invisible), costar un ojo de la cara (to cost a fortune); 10 idioms with production drilling
- Vocabulary: ~30 additional idioms for recognition
- Session-end goal: Student can use 10 common idioms naturally and recognizes 30+ more

**Session 20 — Informal Register and Spoken Spanish Varieties**
- Grammar: Spoken informal patterns across varieties
- Focus: Informal register in Spain: tío/tía, mola, guay, en plan, o sea, venga, ¿mande?; Latin American equivalents: chévere (Venezuela/Colombia), bacán/bacano (Ecuador/Colombia), chido/padre (Mexico), órale (Mexico), che (Argentina/Uruguay), ¿cachai? (Chile); regional pronunciation notes: seseo/distinción, voseo (Argentina, Uruguay, parts of Central America)
- Speaking: Authentic informal Spanish dialogue comprehension; informal register conversation
- Session-end goal: Student can navigate informal Spanish conversation and recognizes major regional informal vocabulary

---

### Week 11 — Written Argument

**Session 21 — Essay Structure**
- Grammar: Structured argumentation in writing; paragraph-level cohesion; transitions; Spanish essay conventions
- Writing focus: Two-paragraph response to an opinion prompt using B1 grammar range; introducción (with thesis) + desarrollo (argument + counterargument acknowledgment) + conclusión
- Session-end goal: Student produces a structurally correct 2-paragraph Spanish argument

**Session 22 — Peer Editing and Self-Correction**
- Grammar: Common B1 Spanish writing errors: subjunctive avoidance or wrong form, por/para confusion, ser/estar lapses under pressure, false cognates
- Writing focus: Revision and error correction of a piece; peer feedback
- Session-end goal: Student can identify and correct their own B1-level errors in writing

---

### Week 12 — Review and Final Performance

**Session 23 — Integrated Oral Practice**
- Focus: Full integrated debate and discussion; spontaneous topics across professional, cultural, and social domains; fluency and repair strategies
- Speaking: Debate format; free conversation; timed speaking tasks

**Session 24 — Final Assessment and B2 Preview**
- Final performance tasks (see below)
- Preview of B2 structures: imperfecto de subjuntivo in more complex contexts, passive subjunctive, literary register
- Independent study resources for B2 preparation

## Final Performance Task

**Part A — Structured debate (7–10 minutes):**
Student presents and defends a position on one of: technology and privacy, immigration, environmental policy, or a social issue with different framings in Spain vs. Latin America. Must deploy subjunctive, conditional, and complex relatives naturally. Assessed on argumentation structure, grammar range, and register.

**Part B — Written essay (200–250 words):**
Two-paragraph argument with thesis, development (with counterargument acknowledgment), and conclusion. Graded on structure, grammar sophistication, vocabulary range, and register consistency.

## Readiness Criteria for B2

A student is ready for B2 when they can:

- [ ] Use present subjunctive accurately with 8+ common trigger types
- [ ] Construct si-clause hypotheticals in both present and past forms
- [ ] Sustain a structured 5-minute argument without preparation
- [ ] Read an authentic Spanish text with ~80% comprehension
- [ ] Write a cohesive 200+ word essay
- [ ] Understand Spanish at near-natural speed with minimal repetition

## Instructor Notes

- **Imperfecto de subjuntivo formation (Session 8) requires explicit time.** The derivation from preterite 3rd plural is powerful but non-obvious. Walk through it step by step: hablaron → habla- + ra/-ras/-ra/-ramos/-rais/-ran. Then tuvieran → tuvie- + ra... Then fueran → fue- + ra... Spend 20 minutes on formation before doing any usage work.
- **Conditional in the si-clause is never correct in standard Spanish.** *Si tendría — this error is extremely common, even in advanced learners. Drill it explicitly in Session 8 and return to it every time a si-clause appears.
- **Cuando + subjunctive for future events (Session 5):** This is the highest-frequency subjunctive trigger in everyday spoken Spanish, and the most commonly made error. Students use indicative out of habit: *Cuando llegues (correct) vs. *Cuando llegas (wrong for future). Devote real drilling time here; error correction alone will not fix it.
- **Se pasivo vs. se impersonal (Session 12):** The key distinction: se pasivo involves a noun that can be the grammatical subject (se vende una casa vs. se venden casas — verb agrees); se impersonal refers to unspecified human agents (se dice que, always singular). This distinction is tested in professional writing; address it clearly.
- **Lo que is extremely high-frequency in natural Spanish** (higher than its French counterpart ce que). Introduce it early in Session 11 and encourage its use from that point forward.
- **Regional framing in Week 9:** When discussing social issues, ensure both Spain and Latin American contexts are represented. Immigration, environment, and inequality are discussed differently in Spain than in Mexico, Argentina, or Colombia. These differences are linguistically and culturally valuable and should not be flattened.
