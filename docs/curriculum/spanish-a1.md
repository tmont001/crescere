# Spanish A1 — Beginner

**Language:** Spanish
**Level:** A1 (CEFR Beginner)
**Course ID:** es-a1
**Status:** Approved draft — not yet taught

---

## Course Purpose

Builds a complete foundation for students who have never studied Spanish or who are returning after years away. The central challenge of Spanish A1 — and the feature that most distinguishes it from French A1 — is **ser vs. estar**: two verbs where English uses only one ("to be"), whose distinction governs how students describe themselves, their world, and their states. This distinction is addressed explicitly and early, and returned to throughout the course. Students leave with enough Spanish to handle real situations and with the foundational competency for A2 past-tense work.

---

## Ideal Student Profile

Absolute beginner or returning learner. Motivated by travel, work, cultural connection, or heritage. No prior Spanish required. May have studied Spanish briefly in school but retains little. Wants to speak, not just recognize words.

---

## Prerequisites

None.

---

## End-of-Course Outcomes

By the end of Spanish A1, students can:

- [ ] Use ser for identity, origin, profession, and permanent or essential description
- [ ] Use estar for states, conditions, emotions, and location
- [ ] Distinguish ser from estar with meaning-changing adjectives
- [ ] Conjugate regular -ar, -er, and -ir verbs in the present tense
- [ ] Use high-frequency irregular verbs: tener, ir, venir, hacer, querer, poder, saber, decir
- [ ] Use reflexive verbs in the present tense
- [ ] Handle transactional exchanges: introductions, ordering food, asking directions, shopping
- [ ] Describe themselves, their family, daily routine, and home
- [ ] Use numbers, dates, and time expressions accurately
- [ ] Use ir a + infinitivo (near future); tener que / hay que (obligation); poder (ability)
- [ ] Use the gustar and doler structures (me gusta/n, me duele/n)
- [ ] Understand slow, clear spoken Spanish on familiar topics
- [ ] Write correct simple sentences on familiar topics (8–10 sentences)

## 24-Session Breakdown

### Week 1 — Sounds and Greetings

**Session 1 — Spanish Sounds and First Greetings**
- Grammar: Ser — full present: soy/eres/es/somos/sois/son; formal (usted) vs. informal (tú) introduced
- Speaking: Spanish pronunciation: the five pure vowels, consonant sounds (r/rr, ll/y, j, ñ, c/z regional variation), syllable stress; formal and informal greetings; ¿Cómo te llamas? / Me llamo...; alphabet; numbers 1–30
- Vocabulary: Greetings and leave-takings (formal and informal)
- Session-end goal: Student can greet someone, give their name, count to 30, and say the alphabet

**Session 2 — Introductions and Ser in Use**
- Grammar: Ser for identity and origin: Soy americano/a, Soy de México, Soy profesora; nationality adjectives (gender agreement); question formation: ¿De dónde eres? ¿Qué eres? ¿Cómo te llamas?; numbers 30–100
- Speaking: Full introduction dialogue using ser in multiple contexts; nationalities; professions
- Vocabulary: Nationalities; common professions; question words
- Session-end goal: Student can introduce themselves completely, give origin and profession, and ask basic questions

---

### Week 2 — Ser vs. Estar: The Core Distinction

**Session 3 — Estar: States, Emotions, and Location**
- Grammar: Estar — full present: estoy/estás/está/estamos/estáis/están; key uses: emotions (estoy contento/a, estás cansado/a), temporary physical states (estoy enfermo/a), **location** (critical note: location uses estar even when it seems "permanent" — La Torre Eiffel está en París); contrast with ser for identity
- Speaking: Describing how you feel today vs. who you are; location sentences; paired ser/estar contrast drills
- Session-end goal: Student uses estar correctly for states and location; understands the fundamental distinction

**Session 4 — Ser vs. Estar: Meaning-Changing Adjectives**
- Grammar: Adjectives with different meaning depending on ser/estar: estar aburrido/a (bored) vs. ser aburrido/a (boring); estar listo/a (ready) vs. ser listo/a (clever); estar malo/a (sick) vs. ser malo/a (bad/evil); estar bueno/a (tasty/attractive) vs. ser bueno/a (good/kind); estar seguro/a (certain) vs. ser seguro/a (safe); estar rico/a (delicious) vs. ser rico/a (wealthy)
- Speaking: Extended drills with meaning-change adjectives in real sentences; Mi café está frío; Estoy bien; Soy médico
- **This week is the foundational block of Spanish A1. Return to it throughout the course.**
- Session-end goal: Student can correctly use ser/estar with at least 6 meaning-changing adjectives and explain the distinction

---

### Week 3 — Regular Verbs and Daily Routine

**Session 5 — Regular -ar Verbs**
- Grammar: Regular -ar conjugation: hablar → hablo/hablas/habla/hablamos/habláis/hablan; negation: no + verb; high-frequency -ar verbs: hablar, trabajar, estudiar, viajar, escuchar, comprar, caminar, llamar, mirar, necesitar
- Speaking: Sentence building from verb prompts; negation drills; describing activities
- Session-end goal: Student can conjugate and use 8+ -ar verbs in affirmative and negative sentences

**Session 6 — -er and -ir Verbs; Reflexive Verbs**
- Grammar: Regular -er verbs: comer/beber/leer/comprender/vender; regular -ir verbs: vivir/escribir/abrir/subir/recibir; reflexive verbs: levantarse, llamarse, acostarse, ducharse, vestirse — with reflexive pronouns (me/te/se/nos/os/se); daily routine: ¿A qué hora te levantas?
- Speaking: Full daily routine narration; Primero me levanto, luego me ducho, después desayuno...
- Session-end goal: Student can narrate their complete daily routine using reflexive and regular verbs

---

### Week 4 — Time, Dates, and Family

**Session 7 — Telling Time and Dates**
- Grammar: Telling time: es la una / son las tres y media / a las ocho menos cuarto; 12h vs. 24h; days of the week (no article with scheduled days); months; seasons; ¿Qué hora es? ¿Cuándo es tu cumpleaños? ¿Qué día es hoy?
- Speaking: Time-telling scenarios; scheduling appointments; discussing weekly routine
- Session-end goal: Student can tell time and discuss dates and schedules fluently

**Session 8 — Tener and Family**
- Grammar: Tener — full present: tengo/tienes/tiene/tenemos/tenéis/tienen; tener for possession and family; tener expressions: tener hambre/sed/frío/calor/sueño/miedo/razón (contrasting with estar for some emotions); possessive adjectives: mi/tu/su/nuestro/vuestro/su (and plural forms)
- Speaking: Describing family: tengo dos hermanos, mi madre tiene 55 años; family tree practice
- Session-end goal: Student can describe family using tener and possessive adjectives correctly

---

### Week 5 — Getting Around

**Session 9 — City Places and Prepositions of Place**
- Grammar: Prepositions of place: a la derecha, a la izquierda, todo recto, al lado de, enfrente de, cerca de, lejos de, entre, al final de; article contraction: a + el = al, de + el = del; ¿Dónde está...? ¿Cómo llego a...?
- Speaking: City places vocabulary; giving and following directions using a street map
- Vocabulary: City places (el aeropuerto, la estación, el banco, la farmacia, el supermercado, el hospital, la oficina de correos, la comisaría, el ayuntamiento)
- Session-end goal: Student can give and follow directions using prepositions and city vocabulary

**Session 10 — Transportation and Ir**
- Grammar: Ir — full present: voy/vas/va/vamos/vais/van; ir a + lugar; transportation: coger/tomar el metro (regional: coger in Spain, tomar widely), ir en tren/en avión/en coche/en autobús/a pie/en bicicleta; buying a ticket: un billete/boleto (regional) de ida / de ida y vuelta
- Speaking: Navigation scenario in a Spanish city; transportation conversation; regional vocabulary note: billete (Spain) vs. boleto (Latin America)
- Session-end goal: Student can discuss getting around, use ir + transportation, and navigate a ticket scenario

---

### Week 6 — Food and Eating Out

**Session 11 — Spanish and Latin American Food; Gustar**
- Grammar: Gustar structure: **me gusta el café** (sing.); **me gustan los tacos** (plural); indirect object pronoun precedes: me/te/le/nos/os/les + gusta/gustan; Encantar works the same way; the food is the grammatical subject — this is a counterintuitive structure for English speakers and needs explicit drilling
- Speaking: Spanish and Latin American food vocabulary in context; food preferences; ¿Te gusta...? ¿Qué te gusta comer?
- Vocabulary: Spanish cuisine (tortilla española, paella, gambas al ajillo) and Latin American dishes (tacos, ceviche, arepas, empanadas)
- Session-end goal: Student understands and can produce the gustar structure correctly

**Session 12 — Restaurant Scenario**
- Grammar: Querer — present: quiero/quieres/quiere/queremos/queréis/quieren; quisiera (polite conditional, introduced as a formula); ordering patterns: ¿Qué van a tomar? / Para mí... / Quisiera... / ¿Me trae la cuenta?
- Speaking: Full restaurant role play: ordering, asking about dishes, paying; cultural note: tipping norms across Spanish-speaking countries
- Session-end goal: Student can navigate a full Spanish-language restaurant experience

---

### Week 7 — Shopping

**Session 13 — Clothing and Colors**
- Grammar: Demonstrative adjectives: este/esta/estos/estas (near), ese/esa/esos/esas (medium), aquel/aquella (far — introduced for recognition); color adjective agreement (gender and number); ¿Cuánto cuesta? / ¿Cuánto cuestan?; ¿Me lo puedo probar?
- Speaking: Clothing shopping: ¿Qué talla usa? / Busco... / ¿Tiene esto en azul?; price negotiation
- Vocabulary: Clothing vocabulary; colors
- Session-end goal: Student can conduct a full clothing shopping dialogue

**Session 14 — Market and Quantities**
- Grammar: Quantity expressions: un kilo de, una botella de, un litro de, 100 gramos de, una docena de, un poco de; ¿Cuánto vale...? / ¿A cuánto está...?
- Speaking: Market scenario: buying produce, cheese, bread; comparison of el supermercado vs. el mercado culturally; Latin American market vocabulary (tianguis, feria)
- Vocabulary: Food at the market; weights and measures; market vocabulary
- Session-end goal: Student can shop at a Spanish-speaking market using quantity expressions

---

### Week 8 — Home and Neighborhood

**Session 15 — Describing a Home**
- Grammar: Hay / no hay (there is/there are — does not change for plural); prepositions of location: encima de, debajo de, delante de, detrás de, entre, al lado de, en el centro de; mi casa tiene tres habitaciones
- Speaking: Home vocabulary; floor plan description; comparing Spanish and Latin American home vocabulary (el piso / el departamento / el apartamento — regional)
- Vocabulary: Rooms, furniture, appliances
- Session-end goal: Student can describe the layout and contents of their home

**Session 16 — Neighborhood and Irregular Verb Review**
- Grammar: High-frequency irregular verbs in depth — hacer (hago/haces/hace...), venir (vengo/vienes/viene...), decir (digo/dices/dice...), saber (sé/sabes/sabe...), conocer (conozco/conoces...); saber vs. conocer distinction
- Speaking: Describing your neighborhood; likes and dislikes about where you live; irregular verbs used in natural context
- Session-end goal: Student uses all core irregular verbs without looking them up; understands saber vs. conocer

---

### Week 9 — Plans and Obligations

**Session 17 — Ir a + Infinitivo (Near Future)**
- Grammar: Ir a + infinitivo (near future): voy a estudiar esta tarde, vamos a cenar juntos; pensar + infinitivo (intention); future time expressions: mañana, esta tarde/semana, el próximo mes, el año que viene, en dos días
- Speaking: Planning a week; making appointments; What are you doing this weekend?
- Session-end goal: Student can discuss upcoming plans using ir a + infinitivo fluently

**Session 18 — Obligation and Ability**
- Grammar: Tener que + infinitivo (personal obligation: tengo que trabajar); hay que + infinitivo (general/impersonal: hay que estudiar); poder + infinitivo (ability/permission: puedo ayudarte); combined scenarios: tener que + pero poder / no poder
- Speaking: Planning scenarios with constraints: tengo que..., pero no puedo..., hay que...; scheduling role plays
- Session-end goal: Student can express obligation, impossibility, and ability in context

---

### Week 10 — Hobbies and Leisure

**Session 19 — Sports and Hobbies**
- Grammar: Jugar a + sport/game (juego al fútbol, jugamos al ajedrez); hacer + activity (hago yoga, hago senderismo, hago footing); practicar + noun (practico la natación); distinction between jugar a and hacer/practicar
- Speaking: Describing hobbies; frequency adverbs: siempre, normalmente, a veces, de vez en cuando, casi nunca, nunca
- Vocabulary: Sports; leisure activities; cultural note: el fútbol in Spain and Latin America vs. béisbol in Caribbean countries
- Session-end goal: Student can discuss hobbies using the correct verb for each activity

**Session 20 — Preferences and Opinions**
- Grammar: Gustar reinforced; encantar, odiar, preferir (e→ie stem change: prefiero/prefieres...); comparative structures: me gusta más... que, prefiero... a...
- Speaking: ¿Qué haces en tu tiempo libre? Extended free-time conversation; exclamations: ¡Qué buena idea! ¡Qué interesante!
- Session-end goal: Student can sustain a 3-minute free-time preference conversation

---

### Week 11 — Health and the Body

**Session 21 — Body Parts and Doler**
- Grammar: Doler structure: **me duele la cabeza** (sing.), **me duelen los pies** (plural) — same structure as gustar; body parts with article (not possessive: me duele el estómago, not mi estómago); pharmacy vocabulary
- Speaking: Pharmacy scenario: tengo fiebre, estoy resfriado/a, me duele el estómago, necesito algo para...; describing symptoms
- Vocabulary: Body parts; common symptoms; pharmacy vocabulary
- Session-end goal: Student can describe symptoms using doler correctly and navigate a pharmacy

**Session 22 — At the Doctor**
- Grammar: Deber + infinitivo for advice: debes descansar, deberías beber más agua (deberías — conditional introduced as a formula); llevар + time + gerund for duration: llevo tres días con fiebre; making a phone appointment
- Speaking: Doctor's consultation role play: describing symptoms, giving duration, receiving advice; cultural note: healthcare vocabulary differences (el médico/la médica, el consultorio, la clínica, el seguro)
- Session-end goal: Student can handle a basic medical consultation and follow advice

---

### Week 12 — Review and Final Performance

**Session 23 — Integrated Role Plays**
- Focus: All A1 skills in extended scenarios; pronunciation refinement; error analysis
- Speaking: Combined scenarios: introductions, making plans, ordering food, navigating, shopping, describing home

**Session 24 — Final Assessment and A2 Preview**
- Final performance tasks (see below)
- Preview of A2: talking about the past (pretérito and imperfecto)
- Resources for independent practice

- [ ] Use ser and estar correctly in common situations without hesitation

## Instructor Notes

- **Ser vs. estar is the central challenge of A1 (Week 2).** Unlike French, where être covers everything, Spanish students must make a grammatical choice every time they want to say "is/are." The meaning-changing adjectives (Session 4) are the most vivid illustration of why the distinction matters. Return to them throughout the course.
- **Location uses estar, not ser** — even when it feels permanent. La Torre Eiffel está en París. This surprises students. Address it directly in Session 3.
- **Gustar structure (Session 11):** English speakers instinctively want to say *yo gusto el café. The indirect object (me, te, le...) + gusta/gustan structure is counterintuitive. Spend real time here; errors persist without it.
- **Regional vocabulary:** Introduce Spain vs. Latin American alternatives naturally throughout (billete/boleto, coger/tomar, el coche/el carro, el piso/el apartamento). Do not overwhelm students with exhaustive lists — flag the most common pairs when they arise.
- **Vosotros:** Present the vosotros forms in conjugation tables but clarify they are Spain-only. Latin American learners will only encounter ustedes. Adjust emphasis based on cohort's target region.
- **Doler (Session 21):** Students who have just learned gustar should recognize this as the same structure. Make that connection explicit — it reinforces both structures simultaneously.
